NK5EO

 

Innovation NK5EO

 

Nurses are involved with the design and implementation of technology to enhance the patient experience and nursing practice.

  • Provide one example, with supporting evidence, of an improvement that occurred due to a change in nursing practice resulting from clinical nurses' involvement with design and implementation of technology. Supporting evidence must be submitted in the form of a graph with a data table that clearly displays the data.
    AND

  • Provide one example, with supporting evidence, of an improvement in the patient experience that resulted from clinical nurses' involvement with design and implementation of technology. Supporting evidence must be submitted in the form of a graph with a data table that clearly displays the data.

 

 

Example One: Change in Nursing Practice – Use of Technology to Prevent Burns to Patients

 

Background/Problem
On February 7, 2016, Beaumont Grosse Pointe (BGP) had an unexpected and unplanned incident occur on 2 South, the Neuro-Stroke Unit. On morning rounds, Timothy Pierce, BSN, RN, clinical nurse, 2 South, was completing early morning physical assessments on his assigned patients. During one patient assessment, Pierce noticed a quarter-sized wound with an open blister on the patient’s upper back, which appeared to be a second degree burn. The patient said she had been given a hot pack by somebody during the night for her back pain.

 

Pierce reviewed the patient’s electronic medical record, and it was documented that she had arrived on 2 South the previous evening with intact skin. Pierce therefore surmised that the hot pack had caused this burn. Because of the severity of the situation, he knew that steps needed to be taken to prevent this from happening again. 

 

Pierce approached Pam Lively, BSN, RN, Assistant Nurse Manager, 2 South, who instructed Pierce to report the incident via RL Solution. RL Solution is an internal electronic process for reporting quality and safety issues, concerns and variances.  These reports are reviewed daily by quality and safety leadership and routed to the appropriate leaders for follow-up and review. The reports are also discussed at the daily operation meeting attended by all nurse leaders. 

 

After being approached by Pierce, Lively began investigating the incident by identifying the those involved in the patient’s care and reviewing the documentation in the patient’s chart. Lively immediately began to share the details of the incident with nursing staff members and to inquire about the process for providing a hot pack. 

 

Through these discussions with the nursing staff, Lively identified that it was common practice to warm washcloths with water from the hot water spigot and give these to patients who asked for a hot pack. Clinical nurses and nursing care assistants (NCA) were surprised by the reported injury, but also alarmed since the use of these hot packs had been the practice on the unit for an undetermined length of time. The BGP procedure for heating pads was to use a K-pad, which is an electric, temperature-controlled moist heat device. However, nursing staff members said the K-pads were difficult to obtain and frequently non-functional when they were, and that the unit lacked the distilled water required for use with the K-Pad.

 

Based on Lively’s conversations with multiple 2 South nursing staff members, it was apparent that new technology and a change in nursing practice were needed. Clinical nurses and NCAs could no longer use moist towels warmed with hot water, but would have to use technology that was appropriate for the condition they were trying to treat.

 

During the afternoon daily operation meeting on February 8, 2016, Lively informed Anne Stewart, MBA, BSN, RN, NE-BC, Vice President and Chief Nursing Officer, of the burn incident, her conversations with Pierce and the need to quickly come up with a plan to address this incident. Lively also said she would talk with the caregiver involved in the incident that day.

 

Lively spoke with the NCA that evening, and it was confirmed that the NCA had used the hot water spigot to make the hot pack. The NCA’s justification was that the K-pads never get warm enough or just do not work, and that it was the practice for the clinical nurses and NCAs on 2 South to use a wash cloth and hot water for patients who request a hot pack. It was clear that this was a patient safety issue and that a plan needed to be developed to prevent this from happening again.

 

Lively and Kim Little, BSN, RN, Assistant Nurse Manager, 2 South, met with Patricia Ackley, BSN, RN, Assistant Nurse Manager, 3 West, and Alexis Rogers, RN, Assistant Nurse Manager, 3 South. Lively shared the incident with Ackley and Rogers because she was concerned that this might be the practice on other units as well. The incident was also shared with other nursing leaders and all hospital leaders during the daily operation meeting, through which it was confirmed that it was widespread practice to use methods other than the approved technology to provide heat treatment.


Lively worked with Becky Frank, Secretary, Nursing Administration, to develop a weekly flyer titled, “Be Aware for Safe Care & Use of K-Pads.” This was then presented at the February 15, 2016 Model of Care Meeting, which is the weekly forum for communication between nursing leaders, educators and nursing administrators. The flyer stated, “if a patient requests a hot pack, or there is a physician order for a hot pack you must order the current technology provided by BGP, a K-Pad.  DO NOT warm a wet towel in the microwave or put hot water on a towel….there is no regulation of the temperature and we have burned (blistered) patients using both of these methods.”     


The “Be Aware for Safe Care” campaign was rolled out to all staff members through the weekly huddle flyer on the same day. This flyer is the mechanism through which nursing leadership communicates information that should be quickly disseminated. It is discussed with each oncoming shift for the following week, and during these discussions the staff provided more information about the use of the K-pads. Staff members said the supply of K-pads was inadequate for the number of patients who request them, and they questioned their functionality. A common concern was that the K-pads would not get hot enough for the patients’ comfort, which over time had led to the use of hot packs as an alternative.

 

On February 18, 2016, Lively asked Deborah Schroeder, BSN, RN, Educational Specialist, Nursing Education, for her assistance in validating what the staff had said about the K-pads. Schroeder randomly selected and tested three K-pads, two of which did not turn on and one warmed up only minimally. 

 

Lively immediately shared this information with Stewart and the other nurse leaders, and Lively informed John Crissman, Biomedical and Safety Manager, of the issue with the K-pads. 

 

Biomedical Engineering conducted a house-wide inventory and confirmed that the available fleet of K-pads was 10 years old and was showing its age in the increased number of reported failures. Because of this unreliability, Crissman believed the appropriate process improvement would be an emergency purchase of replacement K-pads. 

 

The lack of appropriate technology resulted in a patient sustaining a burn-related injury in February 2016. This resulted in a rate of patient injuries related to burns of 1.19 per 1,000 patient days at BGP

 

Goal Statement
To decrease the rate of patient injuries related to burns, as measured by the number of incidents per 1,000 patient days at BGP.

 

Description of the Intervention/Initiative/Activities
March 1, 2016
Lively asked Schroeder to obtain information about replacing all K-pads. A quote was obtained, and Lively presented this to Stewart. Approval was obtained, and Lively ensured that 15 Gaymar K-pads were purchased in February and placed into service in March 2016.

 

March 14, 2016
New K-pads were received. At Lively’s insistence, given the urgency of this issue, Nursing Education was asked to add two new topics to the education plan for the clinical nurses and NCAs. “Hospital Acquired Burn- Case Study” and “Warm Compress Policy 780” were added to the March 2016 NCA education class and the April 2016 clinical nurse education class.

 

March 28, 2016
Lively announced at the Model of Care Meeting that 15 new K-pads had been purchased and were ready to be used. This information was disseminated to all staff members through the weekly huddle flyer titled, “Based on the concerns you voiced, we have purchased 15 new K-pads.”

 

The information about the burn incident was also disseminated and discussed to ensure clinical nurses understood the need to use the appropriate technology to treat patients and to use the appropriate process to report malfunctions of technology so devices can be repaired or replaced.

Outcomes
Clinical nurses’ involvement in the design and implementation of technology led to a change in the nursing practice of providing heat treatments for patients. After upgrading to more current technology, ensuring the necessary supply of this technology and changing nursing practice, the rate of patient injuries related to burns at BGP decreased from 1.19 to 0. Through this immediate change in nursing practice, patient injuries related to burns were eliminated.

 

 

Evidence NK5EO-1, BGP Patient Injuries Due to Burns

 

 

Example Two: Improving the Patient Experience – Use of Vein Finders to Increase Patients’ Comfort

 

Background/Problem
In October 2014, clinical nurses in the Perioperative area of Beaumont Grosse Pointe (BGP) expressed concern regarding the acuity of the patients who were having surgical procedures at BGP. As the hospital census was increasing, so was the number of surgical patients. Physicians were admitting higher acuity patients to BGP instead of to the major health center approximately three miles away. Higher acuity patients have more comorbidities, which can compromise the vascular system and make it more difficult to insert an IV catheter.   

 

Perioperative clinical nurses Karen Thompson, BSN, RN and Christina Spencer, RN were considered experts in IV starts. With decades of experience between them, either Thompson or Spencer would be called to start the IV whenever there was a patient on whom this was difficult; however, they were being asked to perform more difficult IV starts.  

 

During one of these, Nannette Zerb, RN, clinical nurse, Perioperative shifts, observed that Thompson was having a difficult time starting an IV and told her about technology that she had used at a different hospital to start IVs, with great success. The technology, a vein visualization device, improved nurses’ ability in successful venipuncture, decreased the number of attempts and improved patient satisfaction with the process. 

 

Thompson and Simpson approached Deborah Jankowski, BSN, RN, Administrative Manager, Perioperative Services, about the possibility of obtaining this technology to assist with vein visualization for starting IVs. Thompson and Simpson suggested that using this new technology would result in fewer unsuccessful venipuncture attempts, thereby decreasing patients’ discomfort and increasing their satisfaction. Jankowski approached Linda Witt, MA, BSN, RN, ONC, Director of Surgical Services, about the possibility of obtaining the vein visualization technology for BGP.

 

BGP uses Press Ganey as its vendor for measuring patient satisfaction. The Perioperative Services uses the Ambulatory Surgery Survey. In November 2014, the Perioperative Services ranked in the 2nd percentile of the All Press Ganey database for the item, “Skill of nurse starting IV.”

Goal Statement
To improve the Perioperative Services percentile ranking for patient satisfaction with IV starts, as measured by the Press Ganey item, “Skill of nurse starting IV.” 

 

Description of the Intervention/Initiative/Activities
December 2014
Witt arranged for the company AccuVein to meet with Perioperative Services to demonstrate the benefits and use of its vein visualization technology. The meeting was attended by Perioperative Services and Anesthesia nursing leadership, clinical nurses from Perioperative Services, physicians and Materials Management staff. 

 

The AccuVein vein finder would be used in the Pre-Op area on patients for whom IV starts are difficult to improve nurses’ accuracy in initial IV attempts. Thompson and Spencer, the experts in starting IVs in the Pre-Op area, were very impressed with this new technology and believed it would help other clinical nurses in the department with difficult IV starts. Thompson said that when they were called to start an IV, the clinical nurse usually tried at least two times. She said that the clinical nurse being able to get the IV started on the first try would improve both nurse and patient satisfaction.

 

During the demonstration of the vein finders, Darren DeSmyter, AccuVein Sales Representative, explained that the AccuVein would increase patient satisfaction. DeSmyter shared the following information, based on research conducted by Press Ganey:

  • 93% of patients surveyed said they would give a higher satisfaction score if vein illumination was used.
  • 95% said that vein illumination would lead them to consider the clinician more courteous.
  • 94% would have more respect for the clinician.
  • 98% would appreciate the clinician’s extra effort shown by using vein illumination.

 

After the demonstration, Thompson and Spencer advocated for the purchase of the vein finder technology to support clinical nurses in Perioperative Services. On December 26, 2014, BGP received a quote from AccuVein for the purchase of the vein finders.

 

January 2015
Witt obtained approval for the purchase of three vein finders for the Pre-Op, Endoscopy and Anesthesia areas. Witt worked with Cindy Harrison, Director of Materials Management, to purchase the equipment.

 

February 2015
The vein finders were received at BGP. Education on the new technology was arranged by Ruth Roberts, MSN, RN, CPAN, Educational Specialist, Surgical Service. Roberts secured DeSmyter’s services to complete the education on how to use vein finders.  

 

Zerb was a strong advocate for implementing the vein finder in practice, as she had used this technology at a different hospital. As in-servicing was being provided, Zerb and Thompson assisted in trialing the product. They both believed it was an excellent resource for clinical nurses to use whenever they thought they were going to have a difficult time starting an IV.

 

Education consisted of brief one-on-one in-services with the clinical nurses in the Perioperative area. The product was well received by the clinical nurses, as they were the ones who had brought forth the idea because of the difficult IV starts they were experiencing in the Perioperative area.

 

Implementation, including education on the new technology to assist nurses in Perioperative Services with IV insertion, was completed by the end of February 2015.

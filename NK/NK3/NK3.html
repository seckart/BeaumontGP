NK3

 

Evidence-Based Practice NK3

 

Clinical nurses evaluate and use evidence-based findings in their practice.

  • Provide one example, with supporting evidence, of how clinical nurses used evidence-based findings to implement a practice new to the organization.
    AND

  • Provide one example, with supporting evidence, of how clinical nurses used evidence-based findings to revise an existing practice to improve care.

 

 

Example One: Implementing a New Practice – The Peanut Ball

 

In January 2014, in preparation for the opening of a natural birth suite in the Cotton Family Birth Center (CFBC) at Beaumont Grosse Pointe (BGP), Marisa Engel, BSN, RNC-OB, C-EFM CFBC, clinical nurse, began investigating interventions for managing patients who are interested in a natural birth. Engel collaborated with Certified Nurse Midwives (CNM) Wendy Olsen, MSN, RN, CNM and Jennifer Yeamans, MSN, RN, CNM, who provided suggestions for new interventions related to the care of patients in labor. These included incorporating peanut balls into the nurses’ practice. The peanut ball is a new tool being used to assist women during the first and second stages of labor to improve maternal comfort and reduce the length of labor. The peanut ball is a positioning device that is placed between the patient’s legs to open the pelvic outlet to allow more room for the fetal presenting part to descend into the pelvis and exert pressure on the cervix to promote cervical dilation.

 

Evidence-based Knowledge
Engel conducted a literature search and found a small randomized control trial in the Journal of Obstetric, Gynecologic and Neonatal Nursing, published in 2011, that investigated the use of a peanut ball during labor for women who had received an epidural. The peanut ball was used once the patient had an epidural placed, and it remained in use throughout the labor in conjunction with regular position changes. The study concluded that the use of the peanut ball in labor reduced the length of labor, with no adverse neonatal outcomes. The evidence from this study showed that the peanut ball would be beneficial and should be incorporated into CFBC clinical nurses’ practice.

 

Engel presented the article at the CFBC Unit Practice Council (UPC) meeting in February 2014, where it was reviewed and discussed. The UPC members agreed that the peanut ball would be a beneficial intervention, and as a result the CFBC decided to pursue obtaining the peanut balls and then develop a plan to provide education on how to use them. (Evidence NK3-1, Article, Use of a Labor Ball to Decrease the Length of Labor in Patients Who Received an Epidural, and UPC Minutes, February 2014)

 

Later in 2014, an article titled “The Peanut Ball: A remarkable labor support tool,”was published in the International Doula journal. It described how the peanut ball can be used for laboring women to increase comfort during contractions and decrease the length of labor. It also described a variety of labor positions using the peanut ball and how these can improve labor patterns. Copies of this article were posted on the unit as a resource for the clinical nurses.

 

Based on the evidence and input from the UPC and CNMs, Engel approached Patricia Heitz, RN, CFBC Assistant Nurse Manager, about the possibility of ordering peanut balls for the unit. A set of the peanut balls was purchased in August 2014 and received in September 2014. (Evidence NK3-2, Purchase Order for Peanut Balls and Article)

 

In October 2014, Engel attended the Natural Childbirth Workshop, a childbirth education series offered by Beaumont University and taught by Susan Cencer, BSN, RN, Childbirth Educator. Engel attended as an observer to assess how patients were being educated on the use of the peanut ball in labor. During this class, Engel observed the peanut ball in use in a variety of labor positions and learned how to position the patient to promote comfort and fetal descent using the ball. Cencer had clinical experience using the peanut ball with labor patients and attested to their usefulness, saying that she had found in her experience that labor length and pushing time were reduced. The peanut ball is a tool that the clinical nurse can implement to promote continued labor progress and improve the likelihood of a vaginal birth, which aligns with the goal of decreasing the cesarean section rate.

 

Education and Implementation of the New Practice for Clinical Nurses
In November 2014, Cencer presented an educational offering to CFBC clinical nurses about natural birth and the use of the peanut ball for laboring women. During this education session, Cencer gave suggestions for the different uses of the peanut ball for positioning laboring women and allowed clinical nurses to have hands-on practice using peanut balls of different sizes. Clinical nurses took turns lying in the labor bed and practiced the labor positions using the peanut ball. This education was also shared in daily huddles with clinical nurses who had been unable to attend the in-service. (Evidence NK3-3, Sign-in Sheet for Natural Birthing and Peanut Ball in Labor Class November 11, 2014)

 

In December 2014, the new practice of using a peanut ball for laboring women was implemented in conjunction with the opening of the Natural Birth Suite on the CFBC. The peanut ball has become part of clinical nurses’ standard care for labor patients. It is most commonly used for patients who are in bed during the active phase of labor, often once they have received an epidural. 

 

In June 2015, Engel, Heitz and clinical nurses Geraldine Kalkman, RN, C-EFM and Melissa Aughe, BSN, RN, C-EFM attended the Natural Birth: Back to Basics conference offered by Beaumont Health System to improve the care provided to patients who desire a low-intervention birth. 

 

One of the presentations included education on the use of a peanut ball in labor, which reinforced the clinical nurses’ knowledge about providing care for laboring women. (Evidence NK3-4, PowerPoint of Peanut Ball Presentation at the Natural Birth: Back to Basics Conference)

 

Clinical nurses assist the patient to move into a variety of positions, including side-lying, semi-fowlers and hands-and-knees, changing positions every 30 minutes. The peanut ball is positioned between the patient’s legs to help open the pelvis, which promotes fetal descent, optimal positioning and rotation of the fetal head. It is also used during the second stage of labor, while the patient is actively pushing.  

 

When it was initially implemented, the peanut ball was documented on the labor flowsheet under “positioning device, other,” with “peanut ball” then free texted in the comment field. In August 2015, as the other Beaumont sites began to implement the peanut ball, an IT request was made to create a distinct field in the Epic electronic medical record to document the use of the peanut ball for positioning. In April 2016, “peanut ball” was added to Epic as a position device in the labor flowsheet to allow the clinical nurse to document its use. (Evidence NK3-5, Screen Shot of Documentation of the Use of the Peanut Ball)

 

CFBC clinical nurses have reported decreases in lengths of labor and in pushing times, especially in nulliparous women. The patient response has been positive, as they are pleased that the clinical nurse is performing interventions to promote active fetal descent and to potentially shorten the length of their labor. The new practice of using the peanut ball in the Cotton Family Birth Center continues to be used as an effective way to decrease labor length for the patient.


 

Example Two: Revising an Existing Practice – Infant Safe Sleep Practices, Changing from Blankets to Sleep Sacks

 

Evidence-based Knowledge
Barb Sheppard, RN, C-EFM, clinical nurse, Cotton Family Birth Center (CFBC), has over 20 years of experience in administering nursing care to women in labor and their newborns. Sheppard is a strong proponent of the safe sleep initiatives recommended by the American Academy of Pediatrics (AAP), which include:

  • Sleeping supine on flat surface
  • Sleeping in an improved sleep surface without additional items (bumper pads, toys, blankets)
  • Infant never sleeps in caregiver sleep space
  • Supervised tummy time while infant is awake
  • Sleep swaddles to be used in lieu of blankets to provide warmth

 

These initiatives have been a safety focus for birth centers nationally, and especially in Wayne County, Michigan, because of the high rate of death associated with unsafe sleep conditions.

 

In early August 2013, to increase clinical nurses’ knowledge of the importance of safe sleep, Beaumont Health prepared educational materials and a test that was added to the orientation book for all new FBC nurses hired at Beaumont Grosse Pointe (BGP), and it issued pocket reminders for clinical nurses and physicians. This new test was also mandated for staff members currently working in a role that involves providing direct care to newborns, as they have the opportunity to provide patient education about safe sleep guidelines.

 

In late August 2013, Sheppard conducted an informal audit to assess clinical nurses’ compliance with BGP safe sleep practices to align with standards of care. The results of the audit highlighted a need to reinforce education for clinical nurses, who in turn would share the education with new mothers, families and caregivers. (Evidence NK3-6, American Academy of Pediatrics, Interventions Improved Safe Sleep Practices in NICU; Safe Sleep Observation Audit Tools)

 

As safe sleep is a passion of Sheppard’s, she provides education in the community in addition to working with clinical nurses to ensure they are knowledgeable about safe sleep practices. She attends baby fairs sponsored throughout the community to present information on safe sleep, set up models of safe sleep practices and show a video produced through the State of Michigan regarding this dangerous trend affecting our youngest population.

 

BGP is in Wayne County, which has one of the highest death rates associated with unsafe sleep practices in Michigan. The issue of unsafe sleep practices was so important that in 2014 the governor of Michigan signed into law a bill that governshospital requirements related to infant safe sleep practices and education for new parents. A section of bill the reads:

 

Sec. 5887. The department and the department of human services shall collaborate to do all of the following:
(a) Work to improve community-based services available to inform parents regarding the risk factors associated with infant death due to unsafe sleep practices and infant safe sleep practices.
(b) Work with other state and local governmental agencies, community organizations, health care and humanservice providers, and national organizations to coordinate efforts and maximize state and private resources in education regarding the risk factors associated with infant death due to unsafe sleep practices and infant safe sleep practices.
(c) Provide educational and instructional materials that explain the risk factors associated with infant death due to unsafe sleep practices, that include methods to reduce the risk of infant death due to unsafe sleep, and that emphasize infant safe sleep practices.

 

While Sheppard knew the importance of safe sleep practices, it was now mandated at the state level that all hospitals, including BGP, be responsible for educating the caregivers of newborns on safe sleep.

 

In January 2015, Patricia Heitz, RNC-OB, C-EFM, Assistant Nurse Manager, Family Birth Center, was invited to the Wayne County Infant Safe Sleep Coalition. This meeting consisted of many healthcare providers throughout Wayne county who came together to strategize ways to address the sustained high rate of unsafe sleep practices in the community. While at this meeting, Heitz determined that Sheppard would be the perfect activist and an excellent addition to the coalition based on her longstanding passion in advocating for newborns.

 

Facts Regarding Safe Sleep in the Service Area
In April 2015, Heitz and Sheppard attended another Wayne County Infant Safe Sleep Coalition meeting, where they learned some important facts:

  • From 2010-2012, there were 212 sleep-related infant deaths in the City of Detroit. 
  • 2.6 deaths per 1,000 live births in Wayne County, which is twice the overall rate in Michigan.
  • Nearly 90% of sleep-related deaths in Wayne County occurred in an unsafe sleep location, as defined by the AAP (the majority in an adult bed, followed by a couch). 
  • In Wayne County, two-thirds of infants found unresponsive were in a position other than on their backs (the majority on their stomach).
  • Half of the deaths in Wayne County from unsafe sleep occurred before 3 months of age.
  • 90% of Wayne County deaths were on Medicaid health insurance.

 

The areas that healthcare providers who attended the coalition determined to be vital included:

  • Addressing new parents in the hospital
  • Education for grandparents
  • Best practices for train the trainer activities for community members and health providers
  • How to effectively use social media to raise awareness
  • Tummy time and clutter free environment
  • Informational DVDs for waiting rooms
  • Prevention of co-sleeping strategies
  • Need for car seat safety
  • Smoking
  • Breastfeeding and safe sleep
  • Metro Detroit awareness of current status
  • Community resources and awareness
    • (Evidence NK3-7, Agenda and Emails Regarding Safe Sleep Coalition from January and April 2015 Meeting)

 

 

Disseminating Evidence-based Practice
The Wayne County Infant Safe Sleep Coalition’s emphasis was to be instrumental in bringing the current recommendations regarding safe sleep to the community. The attention would focus on marketing. Ideas from the coalition meeting included educational classes for expectant mothers; billboards; and community workshops that target parents, social workers and child care providers.

 

An AAP article from 2013, which discussed recommended interventions to improve safe sleep habits, was also shared at the coalition meeting. These habits and practices have been shown to have a direct correlation to reducing sudden infant death syndrome (SIDS). Across the nation, and at BGP, the movement was to have “every baby, every time” placed on their back for sleep.

 

After the coalition meeting, Sheppard realized that many mothers seen at BGP were practicing unsafe sleep practices with their infants. Discussion with multiple professionals at the coalition meeting gave Sheppard a better understanding of why it is so difficult to make changes. At BGP, many mothers are not looking for new information, but are trusting their parents and friends for their information on caring for their infants. This information can be built on out-of-date, unsafe practices.

 

During this meeting, Sheppard and Heitz learned of a Wayne County initiative that would allow expectant mothers who meet eligibility requirements to receive a Pack-N-Play free of charge to reduce co-sleeping, which is one of the primary unsafe sleep practices. Information about this program is shared with those who meet the criteria when they are admitted for delivery. (Evidence NK3-8, Information Regarding Safe Sleep and Pack-N-Play Eligibility Requirements)

 

Revision of Existing Practice
The information discussed at the coalition led to changes in nursing practice on the BGP Family Birth Center, led by Sheppard. With the staggering numbers of preventable deaths forefront in Sheppard’s mind, her desire for safe sleep practices was re-energized.

 

Beaumont Health policy was changed in March 2015 to incorporate Revised Safe Sleep in Infants. The policy was reviewed by the Clinical Nurse Specialist, Administrative Manager and Chief Nursing Officers from Beaumont Grosse Pointe, Royal Oak and Troy.

 

The changes in practice that were incorporated were;

  • The clinical nurses using a swaddle instead of a blanket
  • The clinical nurses incorporating the concept of “nothing in the bed except the baby”

 

Sheppard learned of a free swaddle program through Halo innovations that would provide the swaddles that BGP would use in place of blankets to reinforce the importance of safe sleep practices. The patients would receive the swaddles that would be used throughout their stay. This practice reinforced to the patient and family a consistent message regarding these practices. Along with the free swaddle program came a focus on the need for education regarding “nothing in the bed except the baby.” (Evidence NK3-9, Policy 6068 Infant Safe Sleep, Less than 1 Year of Age and PO and Information regarding Halo Swaddles Program)

 

Sheppard instructed staff members individually and, as it became available, provided new information on safe sleep guidelines, and information and strategies to ensure that the education reaches those who need it most, the caregivers of newborns. She reviewed the infant safe sleep post-test and education card to ensure that all clinical nurses would have the correct knowledge regarding safe sleep. (Evidence NK3-10, Infant Safe Sleep Post Test and Education Card)

 

To model the behaviors, nurse would need to consistently demonstrate safe practices while couplets are in their care. Sheppard focused on educating the nurses on the need to keep all objects out of the crib and to show the proper use of a swaddle when the first bath is given. Sheppard believed that instructing mothers at bath time would be effective, as they are receptive to bonding and important discussions can be held about infant care while the importance of safe sleep guidelines is instilled.

 

Sheppard continues to serve on the corporate Safe Sleep Task Force and was part of the initiative that led to the “Infant safe-sleep refresher” module, which is required for nurses caring for newborns in Beaumont Health.

 

The BGP Family Birth Center has been an advocate for safe sleep practices for many years. Evidence of this commitment is found in the purchasing of a swaddle sack that is given to every new baby born at BGP.

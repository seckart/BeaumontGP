Strategic Planning TL1EO

 

Nursing's mission, vision, values, and strategic plan align with the organization's priorities to improve the organization's performance.

  • Provide one example, with supporting evidence, of an initiative identified in the nursing strategic plan that resulted in an improvement in the nurse practice environment. Supporting evidence must be submitted in the form of a graph with a data table that clearly displays the data.

Example One: Improving Nurses’ Perception of Patient Safety in the Nurse Practice Environment by Using the Internal Resource Team

 

Background/Problem
One of the goals in the 2014 Nursing Strategic Plan was to increase patient safety on the nursing units at Beaumont Grosse Pointe (BGP) through improvements in the nurse practice environment. 

 

Clinical nurses identified in a survey that the units were safe, but that production pressure would sometimes increase when there was a change in the practice environment due to staffing issues. Production pressure is defined as the feeling of being overworked, such as having multiple patient requests, multiple admissions and overall a very busy day.

 

BGP has used the Agency for Healthcare Research and Quality (AHRQ) to measure the culture of safety on the patient care units. The AHRQ created patient safety culture assessment surveys for hospitals and physician offices/ambulatory settings as part of the goal to improve patient safety in the nation’s healthcare system. 

 

Safety culture surveys are useful for measuring organizational conditions that can lead to adverse events and patient harm in healthcare organizations. BGP conducts the AHRQ survey every six months in the areas of the hospital that provide direct patient care. The entire survey is given in June of each year, and a mini-survey is administered in December.

 

Before the survey can be closed, at least 60% of the nursing staff, including clinical nurses and nursing care assistants, must have taken it. The mini-survey consists of item number five, Overall Perception of Patient Safety, which also appears in the full survey. The questions that make up the Overall Perception are:

  • It is just by chance that more serious mistakes don’t happen around here
  • Patient safety is never sacrificed to get  more work done
  • We have patient safety problems in this unit
  • Our procedures and systems are good at preventing errors from happening

 

Although the Internal Resource Team (IRT), BGP’s float team, does not have a specific practice area, its clinical nurses are still surveyed every six months. It was determined that because the IRT clinical nurses float to all areas of the hospital, their culture of safety score would provide an overall estimate of how well the nursing units are doing to provide a safe practice environment. 

The survey is used to:

  • Raise clinical nurses’ awareness about patient safety
  • Diagnose and assess the current status of the practice environment
  • Identify strengths and areas for improvement
  • Examine trends

 

The nursing staff members are also able to write comments about the practice environment at the hospital and how it affects safety. Clinical nurses’ comments about their perception of how safety is affected at BGP included the following areas:

  • Staffing
  • Production pressure
  • Emergency Center transfers
  • Miscellaneous comments related to safety

 

The full survey measures staff perceptions of the safety culture in the work area, as well as perceptions of the culture of the hospital. 

 

The following areas are covered in the survey:

  • Communication openness
  • Feedback and communication about error
  • Frequency of event reporting
  • Hospital handoffs and transitions
  • Hospital management support for patient safety
  • Non-punitive response to error
  • Organizational learning-continuous improvement
  • Overall perception of patient safety
  • Staffing
  • Supervisor/manager expectations and actions promoting patient safety
  • Teamwork across hospital units
  • Teamwork within unit

 

In June 2014, the IRT’s survey results showed a decrease in the Overall Perception of Patient Safety: 61% of IRT clinical nurses indicated that safety was good, down from 64% in December 2013 and below the AHRQ average of 66%.

 

Wendy Jolley, BSN, RN, Administrative Manager of the IRT, believed these scores needed to be addressed, so she began discussions with the Assistant Nurse Managers of all units to make improvements in the nurse practice environment related to resources.

 

Goal Statement
To improve the IRT clinical nurse AHRQ survey score for Overall Perception of Patient Safety.

 

Description of the In tervention/Initiative/Activities
During a Model of Care meeting in July 2014, Jolley shared the Culture of Safety scores with the Assistant Nurse Managers of all inpatient care units. The purpose of the meeting was to discuss ways to inform the IRT clinical nurses of what the units are doing to improve the nurse practice environment, thereby increasing safety on the patient care units. After much discussion, it was decided that the Assistant Nurse Managers would send the unit staff meeting minutes to Jolley, who would then summarize for the IRT nurses what the units were doing to improve the practice environment. Since the IRT staff members float to all units in the hospital, they were not always aware what was going on each unit. If, for example, a unit that was having an issue with falls changed its practice to have nurses increase their rounding to every 30 minutes, this would be shared with the IRT clinical nurses. 

 

It was very important for the IRT to be kept current on all issues that affect patient care, and it was stressed that the IRT scores would not increase unless the environment on all the units improved. Because the IRT clinical nurses float to all areas, all areas must improve to have an impact on the IRT clinical nurses’ Culture of Safety survey scores.

 

A meal break pilot program began in July 2014, with the theme of “Caregivers must care for themselves.” Meal breaks consisted of two one-hour periods, during which the clinical nurse and their break buddy decide which break they want to take. They can go any time within the hour as long as they are back at the top of the hour. The purpose was to improve the practice environment by always having enough staff members on the unit while also ensuring that all clinical nurses receive a break. Breaks are important since they help clinical nurses remain focused while on the unit by giving them enough time to step away from patient care and give their mind and body a rest, a key element in providing a safe environment. 

 

In August 2014, a Culture of Safety Action Plan was developed by Anne Stewart, MBA, BSN, RNC, NE-BC, Vice President and Chief Nursing Officer, supported by Jolley and the Assistant Nurse Managers. They made the following recommendations, which were supported by BGP:

  • Charge nurses assigned on the day shift on the larger units
    • This would give the nurses an extra pair of hands when the production pressure was increasing on the unit.
  • Equal out staffing on both the day and midnight shift
    • This would allow the nurses on both shifts to have an equal number of patients due to the workload on each shift. This would be a major change in the nurse practice environment, as historically the nursing units always had one less clinical nurse on midnights.
  • Hire additional IRT clinical nurses
    • This would allow more clinical nurse support when the patient census increases.
  • Hire an Emergency Center (EC) pharmacist to support medication reconciliation in the EC
    • This intervention would allow the nurses to focus on patient care. With a pharmacist in the EC to complete medication reconciliation, the clinical nurses on the inpatient units would know that the correct medications were ordered for the patients.

 

In late summer 2014, the IRT moved to its current area, which is centrally located in the hospital so the IRT nurses now feel more a part of the hospital. All IRT clinical nurses were also given individual mailboxes to facilitate communication between them and Jolley. The increased communication and sense of participation increased the IRT nurses’ awareness of efforts across the organization to improve the safety of patients and staff.  

 

From September 2014 through November 2014, Jolley held staff meetings and individual meetings with all IRT clinical nurses focusing on what was being done at BGP to improve the nursing practice environment and thereby improve patient safety. The following changes to the nursing practice environment were reviewed:

  • Adding a charge nurse
  • Adding midnight staffing
  • Increasing the size of the IRT

 

The discussions also focused on safety measures that were put in place to ensure that clinical nurses are practicing in a safe nursing environment. During the staff meeting, Jolley reviewed what BGP had already accomplished to ensure a safe environment for patients and clinical nurses. The following topics were reviewed at the staff meeting to demonstrate ways that BGP provides a safe environment:

  • Bar code medication administration – Assists with preventing medication errors
  • Bed alarms – Assist with preventing patient falls
  • Video monitoring rooms – Assist with preventing patient falls
  • Smart IV pumps – Ensure patients receive the correct does of medication
  • Pyxis medication dispenser – Assists with preventing medication errors
  • Fall alert – Assists with preventing falls
  • RL safety reporting – A confidential way to report safety concerns
  • Electronic medical records – Provide access to the patient clinical record
  • Hand hygiene dispensers – Provide a clean environment for the patients and clinical nurses

Example Two: Improvement in Clinical Practice – “It Takes Two” Nurse/Physician Rounding

 

Background/Problem
In November 2015, Kimberly Little, BSN, RN, Assistant Nurse Manager of 2 South, went to the 2015 Press Ganey National Client Conference, where she attended a session on improving nurse/physician communication. The seminar, based on nurse/physician rounding, demonstrated that the patient and nurse both hearing what the physician says decreases miscommunication and misunderstanding, increases efficiency and patient satisfaction with communication scores for nurses and physicians, and makes patients feel like everyone is on the same page.

 

Each year at Beaumont Grosse Pointe (BGP), nurse leaders gather at a retreat to set goals and develop strategic plans for the next year. Anne Stewart, MBA, BSN, RNC, NE-BC, Vice President and Chief Nursing Officer, sends an email to randomly selected clinical nurses to obtain feedback on the following question:

  • From your perspective, can you tell me what you, as a nurse, want for your patients and your work environment at Beaumont Grosse Pointe?

The 2016 Nurse Leader Retreat was held on November 30, 2015, and comments from clinical nurses surveyed were reviewed. The following theme emerged from clinical nurses’ written comments to the nurse leaders:

  • Clinical nurses wanted to improve nurse/physician communication to facilitate communication with the patients.

 

The clinical nurses wanted improved communication with the physicians so they could improve the care they were providing to the patients by reinforcing the same information the physicians were giving to the patients. This was consistent with the Nursing Strategic Plan goal to improve patient safety.

 

Every Tuesday, BGP holds a customer service meeting to review the HCAHPS scores for the previous week received from Press Ganey and other issues related to customer service. The meeting is for leaders and clinical staff members from all areas of the hospital who can attend. The January 12, 2016 customer service meeting included a discussion about the low scores for the HCAHPS Nurse Communication and Physician Communication domains, with the participants seeing the data indicating a problem with communication from nurses and physicians.

 

For example, in fourth quarter 2015, 2 South ranked in the 26th percentile for the HCAHPS Press Ganey “Communication with Nurses” domain. The percentile ranking in the Press Ganey database is a comparison of the unit’s score within the benchmarking group; ranking in the 26th percentile means that 74% of hospitals in the database are performing at a higher level than 2 South.

 

Little was lobbying for 2 South to be chosen for this improvement project, as she was impressed by the Press Ganey conference presentation that showed an improvement in communication scores resulting from nurse/physician communication. 2 South is a large medical unit that has a high response rate for the Press Ganey Surveys, and Little knew that the unit had one of BGP’s lowest scores in the communication domain.

 

HCAHPS data also included patient comments that were shared weekly with leaders to review and follow up with the patients for service recovery. It was reported that the patients’ perception was that the physicians did not take the time to explain things in terms they understood.

 

Another concern was the number of phone calls made by patients to the charge nurses and/or Assistant Nurse Managers about their plan of care following discharge. Many patients said they did not understand the physician or that nurses were having a difficult time contacting the physicians regarding their plan of care for the day. When informed of this, clinical nurses said that if they understood the plan of care from the physicians this would help them improve nursing practice in giving patients accurate information.

 

Based on the above information, Little decided that she would implement what she learned at the Press Ganey conference to improve the scores for the Communication with Nurses domain.  

 

The Communication with Nurses domain consists of three questions:

  • During this hospital stay, how often did nurses treat you with courtesy and respect?
  • During this hospital stay, how often did nurses listen carefully to you?
  • During this hospital stay, how often did nurses explain things in a way you could understand?

 

It was clear that a plan was needed to improve the patients’ perception of physicians and nurses communicating efficiently and effectively in terms that the patient understands. The intervention would be called “It Takes Two.”

 

Goal Statement
To improve 2 South’s percentile ranking for HCAHPS Press Ganey Nurse Communication domain, measured quarterly.

 

Description of the Intervention/Initiative/Activities
On January 21, 2016, Little proposed developing nurse/physician rounding on 2 South to improve the Nurse and Physician Communication HCAHPS domains. Little met with Anne Stewart, MBA, BSN, RNC, NE-BC, Vice President, and Chief Nursing Officer, to organize a plan for implementing nurse/physician rounding.

 

The proposal was to have five physicians who would participate in the nurse/physician rounding. The physician would arrive on the unit and inform the clinical assistant at the desk which patients they were there to see, in the order that they would be seeing them. The clinical assistant would then call the clinical nurse of those patients to inform them that the physician had arrived and the order in which the patients would be seen. If the clinical nurse was unable to meet the physician, the clinical assistant would notify the charge nurse to go to the patient room with the physician.

 

Key stakeholders for the nurse/physician rounding process needed to be identified, so Little brought the proposal to Pam Lively, BSN, RN, Assistant Nurse Manager of 2 South, who agreed to bring the proposal to the 2 South Unit Practice Council (UPC).

 

On February 10, 2015, Lively and Little took the idea to the UPC, whose membership included 2 South clinical nurses Timothy Pierce, BSN, RN; Annette Gipperich, RN; Michael Hoekstra, BSN, RN; Emily Gross, BSN, RN; and Beth Thomas, RN. The UPC agreed to have the physician/nurse rounding trialed on 2 South.

 

On February 16, 2016, Little reviewed the proposal for the nurse/physician rounding with the Medical Chiefs and Medical Executive meeting for approval. Once approval was obtained, Little and Lively met with Donna Hoban, MD to identify five physicians who she wanted to be part of the nurse/physician rounding program. These were Mark Alrais, MD; Basem Almasri, MD; Manaf Madoun, MD; Alaa Mansour, MD; and Bassam Matti, MD. Beginning February 17, 2016, Little and Lively educated all 2 South clinical nurses on the process and expectations of nurse/physician rounding. Nurse/physician rounding was a major change in clinical practice for the clinical nurses. Prior to implementation of this rounding, the physician would make rounds, talk with the patient and leave. With this change in practice, the clinical nurses would now be active members of the rounding team, allowing them to ask questions, ensure patients’ questions are answered and improve the care provided to the patients. Clinical nurses’ responsibilities include:

  • Going to the bedside with the physician to ensure that the clinical nurse heard what was being said to the patient.
  • Acting as the patient advocate to ensure the patient had their questions addressed.
  • Assisting the patient with clarifying their concerns with the physician.

 

After rounding, the nurse reviews the plan of care with the patient and family to ensure they are aware of what the plan involves. Prior to the rounding, the nurses may or may not have been aware of the plan of care. Now that they are rounding with the physician, the clinical nurses are more knowledgeable about the plan.

 

If the clinical nurse was not able to meet the physician, they were to make sure they informed the clinical assistant. The clinical assistant would then call the charge nurse to ensure that a clinical nurse would be present with the patient and physician. Little and Lively spoke of the nurse/physician rounding process to the nurses and the five physicians involved daily to prepare for the start date of March 1, 2016.

 

On February 18, 2016, Little and Lively spoke with Martha Sejnost-Good, Manager of the Clinical Assistants, to ensure that 2 South would consistently have a clinical assistant at the desk. Little and Lively requested dedicated clinical assistants to champion and assist with the rollout of physician/nurse rounding.

 

The clinical assistant’s role involved multiple steps. Once the physician notified the clinical assistant of who they were there to see, the clinical assistant would notify each clinical nurse in the order the physician was seeing the patients. The clinical assistant would also verify that the clinical nurse would be able to meet the physician in the room. If the clinical nurse was not able to meet the physician, the assistant would call the charge nurse, who would be responsible to round with the physician on that patient.

 

A book was developed to document “missed rounds,” instances of a physician or nurse not being able to make rounds, to identify any barriers that should be addressed. If a pattern of missed rounds was identified, Little and Lively would follow up with individual staff members. Hoban would also be informed if a physician had a pattern of missed rounds for follow-up.

 

In March 2016, all interventions were fully implemented. The change in clinical practice was complete, and nurse/physician rounding began.

TL5

 

Advocacy and Influence TL5

 

Nurse leaders lead effectively through change.

  • Provide one example, with supporting evidence, of the strategies used by nurse leaders to successfully guide nurses through unplanned change.
    AND

  • Provide one example, with supporting evidence, of the strategies used by nurse leaders to successfully guide nurses through planned change.

 

 

Example One: Unplanned Change – Displacement of Administrative Manager in Surgical Services

 

Introduction
The job description of the Administrative Manager at Beaumont Grosse Pointe (BGP) requires a BSN degree. To support all Administrative Managers to obtain a BSN degree, BGP established the Enhanced Educational Assistance (EEA) initiative.

 

The Enhanced Educational Assistance was provided to six nursing leaders who did not have a BSN degree. The EEA included an additional tuition assistance of $6,000 per year in addition to the current $2,000 tuition assistance.

 

On July 15, 2015, the Administrative Manager of the Operating Room (OR) signed an Enhanced Educational Assistance Benefit Agreement stating that he would receive an additional $6,000 per year in educational assistance to obtain a BSN degree by December 31, 2016

 

On March 2, 2016, Randy Whitney, MSA, BSN, RN, NE-BC, Director of Professional Practice, sent an email to the Administrative Managers (AM) in the EEA program inquiring about their graduation dates. All recipients responded with graduation dates no later than December 15, 2016. As the Director of Professional Practice, Whitney was responsible for following up with all the AMs in the EEA program to ensure they completed the educational requirement by the specified deadline. (Evidence TL5-1, Email from Randy Whitney)

 

In November 2016, Whitney contacted the two remaining AMs who had not yet completed their BSN degree to inquire about graduation. One AM responded that she would graduate on December 15, 2016, and the second stated that he ran into some issues and would not be graduating until April 2017. Whitney immediately notified Anne Stewart, MBA, BSN, RN, NE-BC, Vice President and Chief Nursing Officer of the issue. 

 

Stewart spoke with Linda Witt, MA, BSN, RN, CON, Director of Surgical Services, about the information that Whitney had obtained and asked Witt to verify the information with the AM of Surgical Services. The information was correct, and Stewart suggested to Witt that she call a meeting with key individuals to discuss this unplanned event.

 

Unplanned Change
In late November 2016, Witt convened a meeting to discuss the options for addressing the unplanned change that occurred in Surgical Services. The individuals involved were:

  • Linda Witt, MA, BSN, RN, CON, Director of Surgical Services
  • Anne Stewart, MBA, BSN, RN, NE-BC, Vice President and Chief Nursing Officer
  • Randy Whitney, MSA, BSN, RN, NE-BC, Director of Professional Practice and Magnet Program Director
  • Rick Swaine, MBA, MSF, CPA, Hospital President
  • Pandora Walker, PHR, SHRM-CP Administrator, Human Resource

 

They discussed the need for an immediate change in the administrative management of Surgical Services. Witt asked Associate Nurse Manager Kathleen Vandelinder, BSN, RN, CNOR to assume the position of Administrative Manager and gave Vandelinder a couple of days to make her decision. 

 

After careful consideration, on December 5, 2016, Vandelinder declined the offer to Witt and Walker. Walker therefore asked the employment specialist to post the Administrative Manager position. Witt, who was not at the hospital that day, sent an email to Stacey Frazee, Administrative Assistant for Surgical Services, requesting that a memo be sent to all staff in the Operating Room regarding the Administrative Manager position.

 

Guiding Nurses through Change
Witt knew this was going to be a difficult change for the OR staff, as the Administrative Manager, D.F., was a well-respected leader in the OR and in the hospital. D.F. started his career as a circulating nurse and was promoted to the AM position based on his ideas and working relationship with the clinical nurses and surgical technologist in the department. Witt determined that honesty about the situation was the only way the staff would accept this unplanned change. 

 

On December 6, 2016, Witt asked the AM to send an email to the staff informing them of his decision to step down as Administrative Manager of the ORs. D.F. was very forthcoming in his email about the need for him to step down because he had not obtained his BSN. 

 

With Witt’s assistance, he explained to the staff that he was aware of the requirements, stressing that “there are no hard feelings on my behalf in any capacity and that I have enjoyed nearly ‘every’ moment of being manager of your OR.” (Evidence TL5-2, Email from Vandelinder to Witt, Email from DF to Administration and Witt to Operating Staff)

 

Strategies
With the Administrative Manager stepping down, Witt needed to implement strategies to support nurses and the department during this time of unplanned change in leadership.   

 

Communication
On December 15, 2016, during a Surgical Services Leadership meeting, Witt discussed her interim plan for the management of operations of the department. The duties of the Administrative Manager would be distributed to other members of the surgical leadership team. Witt informed the OR staff during daily huddles that she would now be responsible for the day to day operations of the unit and any performance issues for the staff.

 

Witt held ongoing meetings with the Surgical Services staff regarding issues that occurred in the OR. For example, when concerns about room turnover were raised, Witt sent an email to the nursing staff to remind them of the importance of ensuring that the rooms are being turned over in a timely manner. (Evidence TL5-3, December 15, 2016 Surgical Service Meeting Minutes, January 9, 2017 Memo Announcing DF Stepping Down and Memos regarding Day to Day Operations) (The memo was sent in January because of the holiday.)

 

Witt demonstrated her leadership during the transition by providing guidance to nurses on operational issues including:

  • Communication regarding suicide prevention awareness for the clinical nurses in preparation for an upcoming Joint Commission visit. Witt guided the nurses in the expectations for the clinical nurses in the Surgery area and highlighted two policies that the nurses should be aware of.
  • Communication regarding assisting the physician to ensure that all patients who leave the PACU have a brief operative note. Witt explained the issues and asked the clinical nurses to lead this effort to ensure the patients received the appropriate care.

 

Responding to Clinical Nurses’ Feedback
The on-call in surgery provides one example of Witt guiding the clinical nurses. As a Level 3 Trauma Center, BGP is required to be able to open a surgical suite within 30 minutes after a Level 1 trauma presents at the Emergency Center. A number of clinical nurses expressed concern about the 30-minute requirement. To address their concerns regarding this requirement, Witt reviewed alternative plans with Rick Swaine, MBA, MSF, CPA, Hospital President, and will keep the clinical nurses informed of the plans to address this issue. (Evidence TL5-4, Email from Witt to Surgery Nurses dated 6/27/17, 6/6/17 and 5/30/17)

 

During this unplanned change, Witt was very visible to the nurses in Surgical Services and was therefore able to assist nurses with issues that arose. Examples included:

  • Assisting Kristen Levi, RN, CNOR, clinical nurse, with the knowledge of a guest who would be observing a physician during a surgery
  • Assisting Levi with the equipment needs of a physician related to an upcoming surgical procedure
  • Assisting Laura Gleason, BSN, RN, Clinical Infection Preventionist, with appropriate classification of a specimen
    (Evidence TL5-5, Email from Witt to Nurses dated 4/24/17, 6/22/17 and 4/4/17)

 

Through the strategies of visible leadership, communication and addressing the needs of clinical nurses, Witt guided the Surgical Services nursing staff through the unplanned change in leadership of the Operating Room. The Administrative Manager position remains unfilled at this time.

 

 

Example Two: Planned Change – Implementation of Web Scheduler

 

In September 2015, a variety of scheduling practices were used in the Beaumont Grosse Pointe (BGP) Nursing Department. All inpatient units used a paper and pencil schedule; some were self-scheduling, others had a master schedule model and some had scheduling teams as a subgroup of the Unit Practice Council. No matter which model was used, however, there was dissatisfaction among the clinical nurses and nursing care assistants about scheduling practices.

 

The Internal Resource Team (IRT), the contingent staff, used a software scheduling program called “Web Scheduler,” which is part of the ANSOS One Staff staffing and scheduling program. The use of Web Scheduler was initiated with the IRT in 2013 to accommodate this flexible workforce. With this program, staff can view the open shifts on the schedule and fill in where there is a need. A rollout of Web Scheduler to inpatient units was discussed numerous times over the years, but there was no momentum to take on this project.

 

Nurse Leader
In September 2015, Randy Whitney, MSA, BSN, RN, NE-BC, Director Professional Practice and Magnet Program Director, transferred to BGP from Beaumont Royal Oak.  Whitney’s scope of responsibilities at BGP included:

  • Nursing Education
  • Nursing Business Office
  • Internal Resource Team
  • Magnet Program


Whitney was very aware of his responsibilities, as these were the same departments he was responsible for at Beaumont Royal Oak. While he was orientating to his new role at BGP, the issue of scheduling surfaced on various units of the hospital. The issues that were identified were:

  • The delay in posted schedules
  • The delay in getting switches approved
  • General errors in the schedules

 

Clinical nurses were bringing these issues to Whitney because he was responsible for the Nursing Business Office, which completed the scheduling process for the hospital.

 

One of Whitney’s goals that was identified on his 2015 Leadership Appraisal was to review and make recommendations to enhance the Nursing Business Office by April 1, 2016. One way of enhancing the Nursing Business Office was to implement Web Scheduler, as this program would streamline its work. (Evidence TL5-6 Whitney’s 2015 Leadership Appraisal)

 

In December 2015, Whitney met with Anne Stewart, MBA, BSN, RN, NE-BC, Vice President and Chief Nursing Officer, to discuss the possibility of all nursing staff using Web Scheduler. Whitney shared with Stewart some of the comments he had been hearing from the nursing staff regarding the scheduling process. 

 

Whitney told her that, at Beaumont Royal Oak, he had been instrumental in implementing the Web Scheduler program. Stewart supported the concept and suggested that the idea be presented to the Nursing Leadership Team to gain support. Whitney presented the idea informally to the leaders after a daily huddle, and all were supportive. There was some hesitation because they were not familiar with the program, its benefits and its use.

 

Strategies used for Planned Change 
Leadership Support/Approval and Development of an Implementation Plan
Whitney’s first strategy was to ensure that the Administrative Manager and Assistant Nurse Managers were aware of the program capabilities and how it would benefit them and the nursing staff. On February 8, 2016, Whitney arranged a four-hour meeting with representatives from the ANSOS group to give an in-service on the Web Scheduler program. The in-service was held at the Lakeland building from 1 to 5 p.m. 

 

Whitney also reached out to Patricia Avery, Clinical Information Support at Beaumont Royal Oak, to get a copy of the staff version user manual that was distributed to the staff at Beaumont Royal Oak. (Evidence TL5-7, Email Regarding In-Service, Handouts and Web Scheduler Manual)

 

After seeing the benefits of Web Scheduler, the Nursing Leadership Team agreed that this change in the scheduling process should be approved and moved forward for implementation at BGP.

 

In January 2016, after the in-service, Whitney met with Stewart to share thoughts from the Nursing Leadership Team and to develop a plan to move forward with the planned change. The objective of this planned change was to change the scheduling process for clinical nurses and the Nursing Leadership Team.

 

Engaging External Experts
Whitney knew this was a larger project than he could handle, so he approached Stewart about engaging McKesson to provide the education to nurse leaders and clinical nurses on the functions of Web Scheduler. On February 22, 2016, a call was held with Jeffrey Hibbard, Account Executive from McKesson, to discuss entering into an agreement with McKesson to provide the education.

 

Funding was approved, and on April 15, 2016, BGP executed a contract with McKesson to provide education to convert to Web Scheduler. (Evidence TL5-8, Email, Contract, Invoice and Purchase Order for Education for Web Scheduler)

 

Providing Comprehensive Education to Key Stakeholders
Whitney developed and facilitated a comprehensive education program for nurse leaders and clinical nurses prior to the rollout of Web Scheduler. The education was held at BGP in the IT training room at various times of the day to ensure that all staff members who were interested in the program could attend. Education was provided on both the day shift and afternoon shifts. Whitney attended most of the sessions to answer any questions nurse leaders or clinical staff had regarding the new program and changes. The benefits of the planned change were:

  • All scheduled requests would be in the computer and the clinical nurse could track the progress of the request.
  • When the clinical nurse wanted to change a work day, they could send the request to multiple clinical nurses at the same time requesting someone to switch instead of the current practice of verbally asking someone to switch.
  • The clinical nurse is notified automatically of the approval or denial of the request.
  • If two clinical nurses switch days, and they are even switch without overtime, the switch would automatically occur without manager approval.
  • Web Scheduler is a web-based program so clinical nurses are able to access it from their computer at home.

 

Based on the suggestion of clinical nurses during the training, Whitney reached out to Marti Sejnost-Good, Supervisor Nursing Business Office, to develop training material for the NBO staff to prepare them to use Web Scheduler. In addition to the Staff Version User Manual previously mentioned, Sejnost-Good developed, at the request of Whitney, two handouts with screen shots on how to view schedule and swap shifts.

 

Whitney also arranged for the education for the nurse leaders to prepare them for their responsibilities with Web Scheduler. The nurse leader education included the following agenda items:

  • Employee Role Function
  • Administrator Role Function
  • Manager Functions

 

Engaging Super-Users
Super-users were employed to provide peer-to-peer education and support through the change. The super-users would assist other staff members as needed. The super-user could be a charge nurse, clinical nurse working on the professional ladder or nursing care assistant. The training for super-users was held on June 9 and June 10, 2016.

 

Whitney was responsible to ensure there were adequate numbers of super-users on each shift. The use of the super-user strategy was based on Whitney’s experience as a nurse leader.  To ensure its success, it was imperative that there were staff members on each shift who were very comfortable with the system to make the transition as smooth as possible. (Evidence TL5-9 Educational Material for Clinical Nurses and Email regarding Nurse Leader Education; Email June 2, 2016 Requesting Super-Users)

 

Successful Planned Change
On June 13, 2016 during the Model of Care Meeting, Whitney discussed the final rollout plans for Web Scheduler. The Model of Care Meeting is a weekly meeting that the entire nursing leadership attends to review issues that affect the hospital. It was decided that September 4, 2016 would be the most logical date for the rollout. This was the beginning of a new schedule and would work well with the current scheduling. (Evidence TL5-10, Agenda and Minutes of June 13, 2016 MOC and Attendance Roster) 

 

The target date of September 2016 was decided for full implementation of Web Scheduler. Clinical nurses and leaders seemed pleased with the potential of the program, and many began using the program before the official launch. The nursing department was almost paperless with trade requests before the go-live date.

 

Clinical nurses seemed excited about this technology, the potential to look at their schedule and make changes from a remote area without the need to fill out paper forms, wait for the second person to sign and then obtain the leader’s signature, as this was time consuming and fraught with potential for human error. With Web Scheduler, this is completed electronically and the approvals are sent via email.  Additionally, this is also a satisfier for the staff of the Nursing Business Office, as they no longer need to decipher handwriting and enter the schedule changes.

 

With the guidance of Whitney, a planned change of implementing Web Scheduler on all nursing units occurred at BGP. The clinical nurses and the Nursing Business Office staff have found that the new scheduling process has saved time and improved turnover time on schedule additions or schedule changes.

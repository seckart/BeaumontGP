Commitment to Professional Development SE4EO

 

Nurses participate in professional development activities designed to improve their knowledge, skills, and/or practices in the workplace. Professional development activities are designed to improve the professional practice of nursing or patient outcomes, or both. May include interprofessional activities.
Does not include orientation-related education.

  • Provide one example, with supporting evidence, of nurses' participation in a professional development activity that demonstrated an improvement in knowledge, skills, and/or practices for professional registered nurses. Supporting evidence must be submitted in the form of a graph with a data table that clearly displays the data.
    AND

  • Provide one example, with supporting evidence, of nurses' participation in a professional development activity that was associated with an improvement in a patient care outcome. Supporting evidence must be submitted in the form of a graph with a data table that clearly displays the data.

 

 

 

Example One: Professional Development to Improve Practice – Improving Skin-to-Skin Contact for the Mother and Baby in the Family Birth Center  

 

Background/Problem
In March 2012, the Beaumont Grosse Pointe (BGP) Family Birth Center (FBC) attained the World Health Organization (WHO) Baby-Friendly Designation. The Baby-Friendly Hospital Initiative (BFHI) is a worldwide program created by WHO and the United Nations Children’s Fund (UNICEF) in 1991 to encourage and recognize facilities that engage in evidence-based practices for infant feeding and mother-baby bonding.  BFHI focuses on education and supporting mothers to ensure positive breastfeeding outcomes given the proven, extensive benefits of breastfeeding for mothers, babies and communities. The goal of the Baby-Friendly Hospital Initiative is to give all mothers the information, confidence and skills needed to successfully initiate and continue breastfeeding their babies or feeding formula safely, and it gives special recognition to hospitals that do this.

 

To achieve and maintain Baby-Friendly designation, the FBC must adhere to the Ten Steps to Successful Breastfeeding outlined by Baby-Friendly USA:

  • Have a written breastfeeding policy that is routinely communicated to all health care staff
  • Train all healthcare staff in the skills necessary to implement this policy
  • Inform all pregnant women about the benefits and management of breastfeeding
  • Help mothers to initiate breastfeeding within a half hour of birth
  • Show mothers how to breastfeed, and how to maintain lactation even if they should be separated from their infants
  • Give newborn infants no food or drink other than breastmilk, unless medically indicated
  • Practice rooming in, allows mothers and infants to remain together 24 hours a day
  • Encourage breastfeeding on demand
  • Give no artificial nipples or pacifiers to breastfeeding infants
  • Foster the establishment of breastfeeding support groups and refer mothers to them after discharge from hospital or clinic

 

Patricia Ashley, RN, IBCLC, FBC Lactation Consultant, is the coordinator of the Baby-Friendly steps and identifies areas in which further training and education are needed. 

 

During the Baby-Friendly visit in 2012, one learning need that was identified was placing the infant skin-to-skin on the mother’s chest immediately after birth. The recommendation for Baby-Friendly’s Step Four, “Help mothers to initiate breastfeeding within a half hour of birth,” is to place the infant directly on the mother’s bare chest immediately after delivery and allow the infant to remain there for at least one hour or until the first breastfeeding is completed. 

 

Skin-to-skin contact is important for the mother and baby because it stimulates milk production and promotes mother-baby bonding.

 

In November 2013, 76% of FBC patients engaged in skin-to-skin contact with their baby immediately after birth. The Baby-Friendly goal for this measure is 80%. 

 

Goal Statement
To increase the percentage of FBC patients engaging in skin-to-skin contact with their baby immediately after birth.

 

Description of the Intervention/Initiative/Activities
December 2013
The multidisciplinary FBC Breastfeeding Task Force meets regularly to discuss ways to improve skin-to-skin contact and breastfeeding. Its members include Ashley; Patricia Heitz, BSN, RNC-OB, C-EFM, Assistant Nurse Manager; Janice Davis, BSN, RNC-OB, C-EFM, Assistant Nurse Manager; Cheryl Gibson, MD, Obstetrics; Kathleen Rollinger, DO, Obstetrics; and Jeanne Lewandowski, MD, Chief, Pediatrics.

 

Ashley presented the monthly breastfeeding data to the Breastfeeding Task Force in December 2013, and she asked the group for input into interventions to improve clinical nurses’ practice of placing the infant skin-to-skin with the mother after every delivery. 

 

The Breastfeeding Task Force decided to initiate a multifaceted professional development activity for FBC clinical nurses to improve the FBC’s practice of skin-to-skin contact. This activity would include education sessions, an educational video demonstrating how and why skin-to-skin contact is important for the infant and mother, and one-on-one education for nurses who were struggling with immediate skin-to-skin contact. 

 

Early January 2014
Ashley developed education materials for use later in the month.

 

Late January-February 2014
Ashley held multiple education sessions at change of shift to educate clinical nurses on how to successfully incorporate the practice of placing the infant skin-to-skin with the mother after delivery. The education objectives included:

  • Importance of skin-to-skin contact
  • Role of the nurse in skin-to-skin contact
  • Correct way to complete skin-to-skin contact
  • Importance of not delaying skin-to-skin contact

 

Clinical nurses were given information about the benefits of skin-to-skin contact for the mother and the infant, including:

  • Enhanced bonding
  • Better infant weight gain
  • Increased duration of breastfeeding
  • Analgesic effects during painful procedures for the infant

 

The education sessions also included instruction on educating the mother and her support person on the practice and benefits of skin-to-skin contact, and on modifying nursing interventions to allow the infant to remain with the mother for at least one hour.Ashley also used a video in the education sessions demonstrating the practice of skin-to-skin after delivery, and clinical nurses who were not practicing skin-to-skin with patients received one-on-one education.

 

FBC clinical nurses completed the mandatory Breastfeeding Competency Quiz, which included an assessment of their knowledge of skin-to-skin practices. As this quiz was mandatory, 100% of FBC nurses participated in this professional development activity during the two months. Ashley was instrumental in ensuring that all clinical nurses received the education. 

 

The professional development activity of teaching skin-to-skin contact resulted in a change in practice for all nurses on the FBC. The clinical nurses were educated on the skill of providing skin-to-skin contact, and Ashley had them complete return demonstrations to ensure they had acquired the knowledge and retained the information that was taught during the professional development activity.

Example Two: Nursing Education Improves Head CT Turnaround Time

 

Background/Problem
The Stroke Team is a multidisciplinary group with members from numerous departments. It includes nurses, physicians, physical and occupational therapists, speech therapists and representatives from Radiology and Laboratory. The team meets monthly to discuss stroke core measures, internal quality indicators, best practices, case reviews and process improvement plans.

 

One internal quality indicator the team reviews is Head Computed Tomography (CT), defined as timely brain imaging through a head CT, which is critical to the rapid evaluation and diagnosis of patients with a possible ischemic stroke.

 

Brain imaging findings, including the size, location and vascular distribution of the infarction; the presence of bleeding; severity of ischemic stroke; and/or presence of large-vessel occlusion, can affect immediate and long-term treatment decisions. Information about the possible degree and reversibility of ischemic injury, the status of the intracranial vessels and the status of cerebral hemodynamics can be obtained from imaging. Obtaining a non-contrast head CT is important for the identification of contraindications to fibrinolysis, and it allows patients with ischemic stroke to receive timely intravenous fibrinolytic therapy.

 

Emergency Center (EC) clinical nurses play a critical role by calling a Code Stroke for any new patient presenting with neurological symptoms and a “last known well” of less than 12 hours. “Last known well” is the time when the neurological symptoms first started, either inside or outside of the hospital.

 

EC nurses participate in the Stroke Champion Program, initiated in 2013. The Stroke Champions are key stakeholders who function as resources and clinical experts and have a strong influence on process and action plans driving the Stroke Program from the bedside. The Stroke Champion role has the potential to improve clinical performance, quality of care and process improvement opportunities. The Stroke Champion Program uses a shared governance model, which supports Beaumont Grosse Pointe (BGP) professional nursing practice.

 

One of the requirements for Stroke Champions is to attend at least one Stroke Team meeting annually to help nurses understand how process changes can affect data and improve patient outcomes. Minutes are recorded for the Stroke Team meetings and shared with the participants. Outcome data is shared with the entire EC at the monthly multidisciplinary team meetings. At the monthly Stroke Champion meetings, stroke core measures, internal quality indicators, best practices and case study reviews are provided. EC nurse Stroke Champions frequently discuss ways to improve CT turnaround time (TAT), with suggestions taken to the EC leadership team and incorporated into practice.

 

Several EC nurses, including Clinical Nurse Specialist Sandra Rizzo, MSN, RN, CEN and clinical nurses Joan Shimko, BSN, RN, CEN; Alfonso Flores, BSN, RN; Sundara Ganti, BSN, RN; and Angel Thomas, BSN, RN, have participated in Stroke Team meetings and shared ideas to improve CT-TAT.

 

When a possible stroke patient presents to the EC, the nurse triages the patient, escorts them to a category 1 bed, alerts the EC physician, obtains vitals, checks blood sugar and then takes the patient immediately to CT scan. A category 1 bed is the highest priority bed in the Emergency Center. Stroke protocols and pathways and the availability of a stroke team speed the clinical assessment, the performance of diagnostic studies and the decision for early management.

 

In May 2016, a review of the data from the Emergency Center showed three stroke patients for whom a Code Stroke was not called. This caused a delay in triage and increased the time for the CT scan to be completed. It was determined that there was an opportunity for improvement, with one of the suggestions being to provide EC clinical nurses with education on the protocol for stroke patients. 

 

A professional development activity was needed to provide education, with the goal of improving the process by streamlining it and thereby decreasing the time it takes for a patient with stroke symptoms to receive a head CT scan within 45 minutes of arrival to the EC.

 

In May 2016, 25% of patients with stroke symptoms and a last known well (LKW) of less than 12 hours received a head CT within 45 minutes of arrival to the EC. 

 

Goal Statement
Increase the percentage of patients with stroke symptoms and an LKW of less than 12 hours who receive a head CT within 45 minutes of arrival to the EC.

 

Description of the Intervention/Initiative/Activities
From June 2016 through August 2016, a series of professional development activities were provided to EC nurses to decrease the time from presentation to head CT for patients presenting with stroke symptoms.

 

June 2016
In response to the May 2016 CT turnaround time, Steve Apigo, BSN, RN, Director of Emergency Center, conducted EC staff meetings and informed the staff to be ready to transport stroke patients quickly for diagnostic testing, including CT. He also began holding daily huddles to discuss any fallout or areas needing improvement, including CT-TAT. This information was reinforced with the EC nursing staff in daily huddles.

 

Shimko, clinical nurse and Stroke Champion, reported at the June 2016 Stroke Team meeting that EC nursing leadership was reviewing all stroke patients with an LKW less than 12 hours and the use of the CT Stroke Protocol.  

 

To assist in educating clinical nurses, Beaumont Health developed the “FASTER” mnemonic for remembering the warning signs and symptoms of a stroke:

    • F=Face
    • A=Arm
    • S=Stability
    • T=Talking
    • E=Eyes
    • R=React

 

Cathleen Solecki, MSN, RN-BC, CCRN, CNRN, Clinical Program Manager, developed a stroke tip sheet for the EC nurses to improve their knowledge of the stroke protocols. The clinical nurses were required to improve their knowledge of the stroke protocols so they would be better prepared to respond to patients who present to the EC with stroke-like symptoms.

 

Solecki developed a Stroke Reference Binder and provided a copy to the EC as an educational resource for clinical nurses. The binder included the following:

  • Stroke Algorithm
  • Code Stroke Page
  • Clinical Practice Guidelines from American Heart Association/American Stroke Association
  • National Institute for Health Stroke Scale (NIHSS)
  • Symptoms of interruption of blood supply to various cerebral vessels
  • Inclusion/exclusion for Alteplase
  • Post Alteplase Worksheet
  • Stroke Tips Sheet and Core Measure Update 

 

The Stroke Reference Binder was reviewed with the clinical nurses during the unit staff meeting and daily huddles. The clinical nurses were expected to know the content of the binder and be able to provide the care that is required to ensure that patients receive a head CT scan based on the established guidelines.

 

July 2016
Rizzo developed a flyer and PowerPoint presentation that outlined the order of care that should be provided when a patient presents to the EC with stroke symptoms. The overall objective of this educational activity was to ensure that clinical nurses have the knowledge needed to recognize the signs and symptoms of stroke and to ensure the patient receives the head CT scan within 45 minutes of arrival to the EC.

 

This education was provided to EC clinical nurses through huddles, orientation and one-on-one. Because of employee scheduling, the education was completed within a month, and approximately 90% of nurses received the in-person education. Those who were unable to attend the live education received a copy of the information and were asked to review the content and see Rizzo with any questions.  

 

To reinforce the education that was provided in the professional development activity, Solecki developed acute care pocket cards that were provided to the EC clinical nurses. The information on the pocket cards included the following:

 

Stroke Signs and Symptoms

  • Sudden numbness or weakness of face, arm or leg – especially on one side of the body
  • Sudden confusion, trouble speaking or understanding
  • Sudden trouble seeing in one or both eyes
  • Sudden trouble walking, dizziness, loss of balance or coordination
  • Sudden severe headache with no known cause

Goal Times

  • 15 minutes - Door to MD
  • 25 minutes - Door to CT
  • 45 minutes - CT, lab, CXR & EKG results
  • 60 minutes - Door to IV tPA

Treatment Windows

  • IV t-PA - 0-3 hours (Select patients up to 4.5 hours)
  • IA (Intra-arterial) t-PA - 3-6 hours
  • Mechanical Thrombectomy
    • Anterior circulation up to 12 hours
    • Vertebral-basilar up to 24 hours

 

On July 31, 2016, Solecki developed a Stroke “badge buddy” with core measures and the FASTER mnemonic to give physicians and clinical nurses easy access to the protocol for stroke patients. A badge buddy is a card that is connected to the hospital identification badge and makes important information readily available to clinical nurses. This was developed to give the clinical nurses ready access to the information they learned in the education sessions so they can recognize the signs and symptoms of stroke and ensure that patients receive the head CT scan within 45 minutes of arrival to the EC.

 

August 2016
To further reinforce the education, Solecki and Rizzo initiated a Mock Code for the EC multidisciplinary staff, using a Beaumont Grosse Pointe volunteer acting as a stroke patient. The “patient” arrived in the EC with right facial droop, right sided weakness and dizziness. The triage clinical nurse assessed the patient, placed him in a wheelchair and immediately took him back to a category 1 bed. The clinical nurse assessed the patient, called a Code Stroke and notified the EC physician. The unit secretary called the Code Stroke, and the stroke protocol was initiated. The EC nurse then took the patient to CT. This process was timed to assess the CT-TAT.

A debriefing was completed after the mock code with EC clinical nurses and the EC tech, EC physician and EC charge nurse. Areas of opportunity were reviewed with the nursing team.

 

August 2016
Adult Stroke Advanced Treatment Guidelines (ATG) were revised and approved by the Corporate EC Policy and Procedure Manual. The ATG guidelines were reviewed with EC nurses to ensure their understanding of the practices.

 

On August 29, 2016, Stroke Narrator in Epic, BGP’s electronic medical record, went live for better documentation of the care of stroke patients.

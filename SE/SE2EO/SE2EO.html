SE2EO

 

Professional Development SE2EO

 

The healthcare organization supports nurses' participation in local, regional, national, or international professional organizations.

  • Provide two examples, with supporting evidence, of improvements resulting from a change in nursing practice that occurred because of clinical nurse involvement in a professional organization. Supporting evidence must be submitted in the form of a graph with a data table that clearly displays the data.

 

 

Example One: NICHE Conference – Improving the Care of Dementia and Elderly Patients on 2 South

 

Background/Problem
The average age of inpatients at Beaumont Grosse Pointe (BGP) is 78 years. To better serve older adult patients, BGP became a Nurses Improving Care for Healthsystem Elders (NICHE)-designated hospital in 2014. NICHE is the leading nurse-driven program designed to help hospitals and healthcare organizations improve the care of older adults.

 

As part of the NICHE program, BGP rolled out the Geriatric Institutional Assessment Profile (GIAP) in 2015. Through this assessment, a gap in knowledge about caring for older patients or patients with dementia was identified. Specific areas of need identified through the GIAP included dealing with the behavior changes and cognitive issues experienced by patients with dementia, and staff education on dementia/delirium to support NICHE goals.

 

Christine Hoffman, RN, clinical nurse, 3 West, has worked in medical/surgical nursing at BGP since 2011 and has been very active with the geriatric patient population. Her interest in geriatrics was strengthened through her participation in NICHE. Hoffman believes that nurses need to be more attentive to the needs of dementia patients and that they are not always comfortable with managing behavior symptoms associated with dementia. Hoffman recognized that the geriatric patient population is the one most affected by dementia.

 

Hoffman shared her interest in geriatrics with Cathleen Solecki, MSN, RN-BC, CCRN, CNRN, Clinical Program Manager/NICHE Coordinator, and said she wanted to be involved in increasing the knowledge of nurses caring for older patients with dementia.

 

In January 2016, Hoffman and Solecki received approval from Anne Stewart, MBA, BSN, RNC, NE-BC Vice President and Chief Nursing Officer, to attend the National NICHE Conference in April 2016. The NICHE conference highlights evidence-based innovations and research, and supports hospitals in meeting the challenges of quality care for older adults.

 

At this conference, Hoffman and Solecki attended several presentations on caring for dementia patients. For example, they attended the Patient, Family, Caregiver session entitled, “Living with Dementia: Patient and Family Narratives to Build Understanding Among Healthcare Professionals,” and they attended a roundtable discussion on delirium entitled, “Decreasing Delirium Resolution Times for the Elderly: An Interdisciplinary Approach.” Hoffman was enthusiastic about bringing this information to BGP and sharing it with the nursing staff to improve the care of patients with dementia. After participating in the conference, Hoffman earned her certification in Gerontology from the American Nurses Credentialing Center.

 

In February 2016, Solecki was forwarded an email from Stewart that had been sent to her from Samuel Flanders, MD, Senior Vice President and Chief Quality Officer, Beaumont Health, regarding an opportunity to receive a corporate patient safety grant. Solecki submitted the application in February 2016 and received funding to plan, implement and evaluate the Virtual Dementia Tour (VDT).

 

It was believed that the VDT would change nurses’ practice in caring for dementia patients. The anticipated outcome of the VDT was that it would increase the clinical nurses’ understanding of the physical and mental challenges that dementia patients face, and that they would use this experience to provide better patient centered care. It was also thought that the VDT program might have an indirect impact on decreasing falls and restraint use. Hospital leadership also believed that the VDT experience would impact how nurses care for all patients by bringing a patient-focused perspective, and that this would have a positive impact on patient satisfaction scores.

 

The goal of the program was to increase the HCAHPS mean score for “Staff took my preferences and those of my family or caregiver into account in deciding my healthcare needs.” In May 2016, the mean score was 80%.

 

Goal Statement
To increase the HCAHPS mean score for the item, “Staff took my preferences and those of my family or caregiver into account in deciding my healthcare needs.”

 

Description of the Intervention/Initiative/Activities
June-July 2016
After seeing information about a Virtual Dementia Tour at the NICHE conference, Hoffman was eager to bring the program to BGP, supported by the grant. Solecki contacted Second Winds, a company that sells a dementia tour program, to inquire about the VDT program and what is required to use their class. Solecki received a contract template and licensing agreement from Second Winds, and the contract was sent to and reviewed by Beaumont Health Legal Department.   

 

August 2016
Solecki met with Stewart to discuss the logistics of the program, including:

  • The cost of the program, including materials and personnel
  • Location for training facilitators
  • Location of the VDT experience for clinical nurses
  • Scheduling for the facilitators and the experience
  • Evaluation of program, and pre- and post-survey

 

September 2016
Solecki received approval from the Legal Affairs Office for the contract with Second Winds, and the contract and licensing agreement were signed.

 

The following clinical nurses and staff members volunteered to serve as facilitators for the VDT. The facilitator training was provided over two days:

  • Christine Hoffman, RN, clinical nurse, 3 West
  • Alfonso Flores, BSN, RN, clinical nurse, Emergency Center
  • Debra Briske, MSN, RN, CEN, Clinical Nurse Specialist
  • Angela Falzarano, MSN, RN-BC, Clinical Nurse Specialist
  • Edmund Neumaier, BSN, RN, Palliative Care Coordinator
  • Sandra Rizzo, MSN, RN, CEN Clinical Nurse Specialist
  • Cathleen Solecki, MSN, RN-BC, CCRN, CNRN, Clinical Program Manager
  • Lisa Hartner, Program Manager

 

October 2016
The first VDT experience, scheduled for November 1, 2016, was planned. Hoffman, Neumaier and Solecki purchased equipment for the VDT experience, which would be open to any clinical nurse, nursing care assistant and direct care staff member. Participants signed up in Halogen, BGP’s online education program. The goal was to have as many clinical nurses and nursing care assistants as possible attend.

 

The experience included the following:

  • Participants took a pre-survey on their perception of taking care of dementia patients.
  • Participants were given an explanation of what the VDT experience would entail.
  • Participants donned gloves to simulate arthritis and loss of fine motor control, black dotted glasses to simulate vision loss, headphones connected to an MP3 player to simulate muted voices and static noise, and shoe inserts to simulate neuropathy.
  • Participants were taken to a dimly lit room to begin the experience.
  • To simulate the dementia experience, the trained facilitators guided participants through common everyday tasks and exercises while outfitted with patented devices that altered their senses. 

 

November and December 2016
The VDT clinical nurse training began on November 1, 2016. This was a hospital-wide initiative, with a total of 224 participants attending the 12 sessions and experiencing the Virtual Dementia Tour. 

 

The tour allowed clinical nurses to experience firsthand what a dementia patient experiences. During the debriefing after the tour, the participants were asked how they were going to change their practice in caring not only for patients with dementia, but for all patients on the unit.  The practice changes included:

  • Giving the patient ample time patient to respond to a task
  • Using a caring touch
  • Cutting down on noise and distraction
  • Redirecting the patient when they exhibit aggressive behavior
  • Allowing the patient to lead the care
  • Giving the patient choices rather than directing them what to do

 

This program was designed to help nurses change the way they provide care for patients with dementia, and all patients on the unit, by ensuring patients’ perspectives and preferences are included in the plan of care.

Example Two: Improving Skin Care and Preventing HAPUs

 

Background/Problem
In December 2014, after the fourth quarter NDNQI pressure ulcer prevalence survey was completed, Anne Stewart, MBA, BSN, RN, NE-BC, Vice President and Chief Nursing Officer, reviewed the nurse-sensitive data on pressure ulcers and noted that Beaumont Grosse Pointe (BPG) was above the Magnet mean for Percent of Surveyed Patients with Hospital-Acquired Pressure Ulcers (HAPU) Stage II and above.

 

Stewart identified through her review of the data that 1.50% of patients had a HAPU stage II or above in third quarter 2014, and that this had increased to 2.00% in fourth quarter 2014.Stewart therefore scheduled a meeting with Renee Mende, BSN, RN, CWOCN, Inpatient Wound Care clinical nurse, to discuss the results of the previous two pressure ulcer prevalence surveys. 

 

Mende told Stewart of her concern that not all pressure ulcers had been hospital-acquired. She believed that some patients were arriving with a pressure ulcer but that there was inconsistent documentation of the four-eyed assessment and of wounds on admission. If the patient arrives with a wound but this is not documented in the electronic medical record, it i counted as a HAPU.

 

Mende said she would research resources from the Wound, Ostomy and Continence Nurses (WOCN) Society and determine best practices for preventing pressure ulcers.  Mende is an active member of the WOCN Society, is certified as a Wound Ostomy Clinical Nurse and keeps up to date on knowledge about all aspect of wound care by, for example, attending conferences. (Wound care nurses also are employed in the Outpatient Wound Care Center.)

 

Another NDNQI pressure ulcer prevalence survey was conducted in March 2015; 2.86% of patients had a HAPU stage II or above, compared to the NDNQI mean benchmark of 1.32%.

 

Goal Statement
To decrease the percentage of patients with a hospital-acquired pressure ulcers stage II or above, measured through quarterly prevalence surveys and reported to NDNQI.

 

Description of the Intervention/Initiative/Activities
April 2015
The Fact Sheet written by the Professional Practice Committee of the WOCN Society outlines the role of the wound care nurse. It states the wound care nurse should:

  • Provide formal education to all levels of staff
  • Incorporate evidence-based practice into the care of the patient
  • Serve as a clinical expert on wound care
  • Participate in outcome evaluation including NDNQI pressure ulcer prevalence survey
  • Benchmark with other organizations

 

Mende reviewed the website of her professional organization, the WOCN Society, to determine best practices for preventing HAPUs. These include:

  • Performing a comprehensive skin assessment, including four-eyed assessment, on admission and transfer
  • Using a standardized pressure ulcer risk assessment
  • Care planning and implementation to address areas of risk

 

May 2015
Based on the information from the WOCN Society, Mende met with Debra Briske, MSN, RN, CEN, Clinical Nurse Specialist, Nursing Education, and Deborah Schroeder, BSN, RN, CGRN, Education Specialist, Nursing Education, to develop a plan for providing inpatient clinical nurses with education on pressure ulcer prevention.

 

Mende, Briske and Schroeder decided that a two-part education series would improve clinical nurses’ knowledge and determined that the RN Education Sessions would be the perfect venue to provide this education. The RN Education Sessions are held four to five times a year for three hours to update clinical nurses on existing and new policy issues, provide education on new products and address any issues deemed critical by the nursing leadership team.

 

The education program would be in two parts. The June 2015 session would provide a brief overview of pressure ulcers and review a case study of a patient who developed a HAPU at BGP, and the August 2015 session would be an in-depth program regarding pressure ulcers.

 

In late May 2015, the outline of the program was reviewed with Pam Lively, BSN, RN, Assistant Nurse Manager, 2 South, and Sue Wolka, BSN, RN, Assistant Nurse Manager, CCSU, who agreed that the program would be beneficial. 

 

June-July 2015
RN Education Sessions were held on the following days:

  • Monday, June 22, 2015 – 3 sessions
  • Thursday, June 25, 2015 – 3 sessions
  • Friday, June 26, 2015 – 3 sessions
  • Monday, June 29, 2015 –3 sessions
  • Tuesday, June 30, 2015 – 3 sessions
  • Wednesday, July 1, 2015 – 3 sessions
  • Thursday, July 2, 2015 – 1 session

 

The agenda consisted of an introduction to new trends in pressure ulcers, a review of a case study and an introduction to the August education sessions at which wound care would be studied further. More than 210 clinical nurses attended the June/July education sessions. It was also announced that BGP is always looking for new clinical nurses to be part of the NDNQI Pressure Ulcer Team, and two clinical nurses signed up to be part of the team.

 

August-September 2015
Mende, with the assistance of Briske and Schroeder, provided pressure ulcer education to clinical nurses during the August education sessions. It was important that Mende teach all sessions based on the WOCN Society’s recommendation that the wound care nurse serves as a clinical expert in wound care. The sessions were held on the following days:

  • Friday, August 21, 2015 – 2 sessions
  • Monday, August 24, 2015 – 2 sessions
  • Monday, August 31, 2015 – 2 sessions
  • Wednesday, September 2, 2015 – 1 session

 

The education sessions consisted of two parts:

  • Lecture: “Must knows” about pressure ulcers, wound and skin injuries: Risk factors, prevention, identification and treatment - 0.5 contact hour
  • Hands on Skill: Wound Care Fair - 1.0 contact hour

 

The objectives of the program were to:

  • Identify patients at risk for pressure ulcers and other skin injuries
  • List basic principles of wound prevention and maintenance of healthy skin tissue
  • Classify pressure ulcer stages and identify other skin injuries
  • Name appropriate interventions to prevent pressure ulcers and other skin injuries
  • Name appropriate initial treatment therapy for pressure ulcers and other skin injuries.

 

The Wound Care Fair consisted of three stations:

  • Name that wound
  • Pressure Ulcer Prevention, Support Surfaces & Documentation
  • Treatment options/Choosing a Wound Care Product.

 

After the program, the nurses were expected to incorporate the knowledge they learned in the program to assist in preventing pressure ulcers. They were expected to perform the four-eyed assessment on all admissions and transfers, incorporate prevention strategies into their practice and begin using the correct products for pressure ulcers.

 

The program was well received by the more than 200 clinical nurses who attended.

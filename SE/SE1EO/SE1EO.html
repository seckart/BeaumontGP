SE1EO

 

Professional Development SE1EO

 

Clinical nurses are involved in interprofessional decision-making groups at the organizational level. (Examples include, but are not limited to, organizational quality councils, budget review committees, equipment selection committees, mortality and morbidity committees, pharmacy and therapeutics committees, blood utilization committees, safety committees, and bioethics committees.)

  • Provide two examples, with supporting evidence, of improvements resulting from the contributions of clinical nurses in interprofessional decision-making groups at the organizational level. Supporting evidence must be submitted in the form of a graph with a data table that clearly displays the data.

 

 

 

Example One: Improving Emergency Center Flow – Emergency Center to Inpatient Flow Team

 

Background/Problem
Patient volume in the Emergency Center (EC) at Beaumont Grosse Pointe (BGP) has been steadily increasing, which has resulted in several challenges including prolonged lengths of stay for patients.

 

Data on length of stay (LOS) is gathered and is part of the EC Core Measures. Core Measure data is used by the Centers for Medicare and Medicaid (CMS) for the Value-Based Purchasing Program, which has a financial impact on the hospital. Core Measures are also performance measures required by The Joint Commission. EC Core Measure data on LOS is broken down into smaller data points including:

  • Arrival to admission
  • Arrival to doctor
  • Admission decision to departure for admitted patients
  • Arrival to discharge for patients being discharged from the EC

 

A multidisciplinary team of hospital leaders had formed a task force in late 2013 to discuss issues in patient throughput, particularly in regard to patients being admitted from the EC when there is a critical shortage of beds in the hospital. This task force examined the EC Core Measure data for LOS.

 

In March 2015, Chris Stesney-Ridenour, MBA, Vice President and Chief Operating Officer, met with Anne Stewart, MBA, BSN, RN, NE-BC, Vice President and Chief Nursing Officer, and Wendy Jolley, BSN, RN, Director of Emergency Center, to discuss the holding of EC patients, bed management and admitting practices.

 

Many processes and work flows were identified as contributing to prolonged LOS in the EC. Jolley presented the issue of prolonged wait times to Steve Apigo, BSN, RN, Administrative Manager, Emergency Center, and Charlotta Marion, RN, Assistant Nurse Manager. Apigo and Marion looked closely at each data point to identify areas with the greatest opportunity for improvement; these included the time it takes to admit and discharge patients.  

 

They decided to focus on admitted patients and examined the following data:

  • Door to doctor
  • Door to diagnostics
  • Door to results
  • Door to admission decision
  • Admission decision to departure

 

After examining the data, they determined that the greatest opportunity for decreasing LOS was in the time from admit decision to departure from the EC. EC leaders requested input from the EC charge nurses and clinical nurses Beverly Gandolfi, RN; Joan Shimko, BSN, RN, CEN; and Sundara Ganti, BSN, RN, and they began to examine barriers to the patient being admitted to the inpatient unit.

 

Apigo completed a literature search to obtain information on ways to reduce LOS, and EC leaders contacted other ECs in the Beaumont Health system to determine best practices.

 

In July 2015, Theresa Ledesma, a project manager for Beaumont Health, was invited to BGP to assist with the project. Ledesma met with Stewart, Jolley and Kristen Cavender, BSIE, MBA, CSSBB, Administrative Director. Ledesma had been working with the team at Beaumont Trenton to decrease its overall LOS. Ledesma, Jolley, Apigo and Marion visited Beaumont Trenton, which had shown progress in decreasing LOS, to examine its processes for patient throughput.

 

Jolley, Apigo and Marion discussed the processes in place at Trenton with EC clinical nurses at BGP. Apigo, Marion and clinical nurses Thomas Difilippo, BSN, RN; Anna Welch, BSN, RN; and Alfonso Flores, BSN, RN then made visits to Trenton to observe the patient flow and processes of the Emergency Department. Discussions were held with the EC clinical nurses to solicit their input into which processes may work at BGP. 

 

In July 2015, EC data showed a time of 109 minutes between decision to admit and time of departure. The goal for this Core Measure was 60 minutes. Multiple discussions about the causes of delays were held with EC clinical nurses, who identified the following factors contributing to delays in transferring patients to an inpatient bed:

  • Incomplete testing and/or results
  • Need for the clinical nurse to complete care
  • Waiting for the Emergency Center physician to enter inpatient orders
  • Transfer and/or discharge of patients from inpatient beds
  • Delay in cleaning empty inpatient beds
  • Delays in central transport
  • Difficulty in EC nurse to inpatient nurse handoff

 

It was clear that it would take a multidisciplinary team to develop an improvement plan. The improvement process focused on the metric of decision to admit time to time of departure.

In July 2015, an administrative decision was made to have a system-level project manager assist with a process improvement plan to decrease the amount of time between physician decision to admit and patient departure from the EC. In August 2015, Ledesma was assigned to be the project manager for the process improvement plan at BGP.

 

Because of the quality and financial impact of this issue on BGP, this organization-wide decision-making group was formed for the sole purpose of decreasing the time it takes for patients to leave the EC once an order is written. The directive from Chris Stesney-Ridenour, MBA, Vice President and Chief Operating Officer, was to develop a process for implementing the necessary steps to decrease the turnaround time. The team was expected to periodically report its progress to the administrative team.

 

Goal Statement
To decrease the amount of time, in minutes, from decision to admit to the time of departure for patients in the Emergency Center.

 

Description of the Intervention/Initiative/Activities
August 2015
An initial kickoff meeting was held with an interprofessional team that included EC clinical nurses, inpatient clinical nurses, and representatives from hospital administration, nursing leadership, Medical Services, Care Management, Registration, Transportation Services and the Nursing Business Office. This team, called the Emergency Center to Inpatient Flow Team, was led by Project Manager Ledesma and had the overall objective to “improve bed placement turnaround times for EC to IP patients to be placed in an inpatient bed within one hour from the time the admit order was received.” The specific goal was to decrease the time, in minutes, from decision to admit to the time of departure for patients in the Emergency Center 

 

The team charter that was developed included frequency of meetings, expectations, commitment and timelines. Process Improvement (PI) methodology was described to clinical nurses for both the EC and the inpatient stakeholders. This organization-wide decision-making group was given the authority to implement strategies to decrease the time patients spend in the EC after an admit order.

 

September 2015

  • September 14, 2015: Jolley submitted an IT request for the development of detailed reports. Jolley wanted reports available in Epic to track internal metrics, Core Measures and turnaround times. This report would be used to note any reduction in the LOS in the department, assist with patient flow and increase customer satisfaction.
  • September 15, 2015: A meeting was held to review and agree on the team members and to review and modify the current state map and begin identifying any barriers. The current state map is the process that is currently being used.
  • September 22, 2015: A team meeting was held, including clinical nurses, to begin looking at barriers and how to get to the optimal state. The interprofessional group discussed the barriers of each scope of practice or discipline, and a process map of ways to implement changes and remove barriers. Clinical nurses provided feedback about the effect these changes would have on their work flow. The team understood that there would have to be buy-in from several other groups, including IT and physicians.

 

October 2015

  • October 13, 2015: The EC clinical nurses addressed the issue of overcrowding in the EC; when the EC was overcrowded it became backed up, which increased the time from admit to leaving the EC. The interprofessional team reconvened to discuss creating and implementing a surge plan, which would consist of action steps to be taken in the event of overcrowding in the EC. The surge plan was a page sent to all inpatient nursing leaders and leaders from administration, Dietary, Transportation and Environmental Services. Under the surge plan, all inpatient clinical nurses would place a priority on discharging patients and transferring admitted patients across inpatient units. Another issue that the clinical nurses discussed was the physicians’ work flow, which the nurses believed sometimes caused backups.
  • October 20, 2015: Glen Clark, MD, Medical Director of the EC, began to attend the Flow Team’s meetings based on the suggestions that an Emergency Center physician (ECP) should be includedto provide ECP practice and feedback. Deb Briske, MSN, RN, CEN, Clinical Nurse Specialist, Nursing Education, and Sandra Rizzo, MSN, RN, CEN, Clinical Nurse Specialist, Emergency Center, were also included on the team to begin a plan for the roll out of education for the project.

 

November 2015  

  • November 10, 2015: Rose Miszkowski, MSW, Director of Patient Registration, and Stesney-Ridenour discussed adding a pre-admit button to the BGP track board, which identifies the status of the patient. This pre-admit button would enable ECPs to pre-admit patients electronically, which would enable Bed Management to clinically review the patient’s chart with Care Management and assign beds proactively. This process would assist in prioritizing clean bed assignments, with the intention of decreasing the admit time to bed assignment and bed assignment to departure.
  • November 12, 2-15- Stesney-Ridnour convened a special meeting to discuss employee roles in the new process, including how to coordinate work flows between all departments. Each professional group was given a role and responsibility for the project:
    • EC physicians: Use the “pre-admit” function in Epic.
    • Attending physicians: Facilitate timely discharge and transfer of patients.
    • Care Management: Look at the “pre-admit” patients and proactively work on bed assignments.
    • Bed Management: Use the RN Care Managers for proper placement of patients. Inform Environmental Services of priority beds to clean. Page the inpatient RN to inform of pending admission.
    • Unit Secretary: Use the transport “appointment” in the bed tracking system. Follow up with transport if the patient is not moved after 15 minutes, place a “courtesy call” to inpatient floor informing them the patient is on their way.
    • EC RN: Make certain admitted patient is ready to go within 20 minutes.
    • EC Charge RN: Find alternative transportation resource if necessary.
    • Inpatient RN: Monitor transfers and discharges for timeliness. Follow bed ahead practice and assign nurses proactively. Check Epic when assigned a patient and facilitate transfers.
    • Environmental Services: Use stat bed cleaning per protocol.

 

December 2015
The last step was to educate the ECPs on the new process. Clark provided communication and follow up on the use of the pre-admit function in Epic, and this was reinforced at monthly provider meetings and through daily spot monitoring.

 

The pilot of the new process began at 0700 on December 21, 2015. The pilot lasted for approximately two weeks without any changes, and the entire process was fully implemented by December 31, 2015.EC leadership as well as Ledesma, Project Manager, were on the units to provide support for the EC and inpatient team. Unit secretaries began the new process of placing patients in the transport tracker for a 20-minute appointment time.

Outcomes
Clinical nurses’ involvement in the organization-level Emergency Center to Inpatient Flow Team resulted in an improvement in disposition of patients with an admission order. After the implementation of the new admit flow process, the time from decision to admit to time of departure for patients in the Emergency Center decreased from 111 minutes in August 2015 to 85 minutes in January 2016, 87 minutes in February 2016 and 85 minutes in March 2016.

Example Two: Improving Beaumont Grosse Pointe’s Press Ganey Overall Rating

 

Background/Problem
Beaumont Grosse Pointe (BGP) uses Press Ganey as its vendor to monitor HCAHPS survey results, which provide it with valuable information on how customers perceive the quality of care they received.

 

BGP is part of Beaumont Health, an eight-hospital system in the Detroit area. HCAHPS scores at Beaumont Health are monitored weekly, monthly and yearly. BGP receives Press Ganey HCAHPS scores weekly to gage how the monthly scores will be reported.  The reports are presented outlining each HCAHPS dimension at the hospital level and by inpatient nursing unit. Each dimension is presented separately and depicted in different colors. If the score is above the target the box is green, and if the score is below the target the box is red. The targets are based on the percentile ranking for the All Press Ganey database.  

 

The target for 2015 was set at the 62nd percentile ranking, meaning each dimension must achieve a 62nd percentile ranking to meet the target. The targets are adjusted yearly based on how well BGP performed. No targets are set below the 50th percentile ranking.  

 

Rick Swaine, MBA, MSF, CPA, Hospital President, monitors the scores weekly and reviews them during Beaumont Health’s presidents meeting. Swaine believes that the question about the “Overall Rating” of the hospital has an impact on all other questions and drive the scores. The question that is asked is:

“Using any number from 0 to 10, where 0 is the worse hospital possible and 10 is the best hospital possible, what number would you use to rate this hospital during your stay?”

The hospital is scored based on the number of ratings of 9 or 10 that are achieved. BGP’s year-end 2014 score for Overall Rating was in the 49th percentile, meaning it did not meet the target. In March 2015, this ranking fell to the 46th percentile.

 

In early April 2015, with scores inconsistent, Swaine determined that something needed to be changed to improve the Overall Rating of the hospital. Swaine called a meeting with Anne Stewart, MBA, BSN, RN, NE-BC, Vice President and Chief Nursing Officer; Kristen Cavender, BSIE, MBA, CSSBB, Administrative Director; and Mary Kay Blaine, Manager of Service Excellence, to discuss ways to improve this score. 

 

Goal Statement
To improve the percentile ranking of Beaumont Grosse Pointe’s Press Ganey HCAHPS score for the hospital’s Overall Rating.

 

Description of the Intervention/Initiative/Activities
April 2015

A meeting was held to review the Press Ganey scores with BGP’s nursing leaders and begin to develop a plan to improve them, particularly the score for Overall Rating.  The nurse leaders in attendance were Marie Reinman, RN, Director Critical Care and Heart and Vascular; Linda Witt, MA, BSN, RN, ONC, Director Surgical Services; and Wendy Jolley, BSN, RN, Director Emergency Center. During this first meeting they quickly decided that, to be effective, the group need representation from across the organization, including other hospital leaders as well as clinical staff, especially clinical nurses.

 

May 2015
Meetings were held in May 2015 with various leaders, clinical nurses and clinical staff to determine the best way to increase scores. During one meeting, Kelly Adams, MSN, RN, Assistant Nurse Manager, Critical Care; Patricia Heitz, RN, Assistant Nurse Manager, Family Birth Center; and Cindy Gregorich, MSA, BSN, RN, Manager, Care Management discussed holding a weekly meeting for all hospital leaders and clinical nurses to discuss scores and develop ways to improve them. 

 

Everyone thought this was a great idea, and plans were made to have the first meeting in June 2015. The newly formed organization-level Customer Service meeting would be structured in a way that all members, including clinical nurses, would give suggestions about how to improve care on the unit and raise any issues that might be affecting the way they provide care. The clinical nurses were crucial to the success of these meetings, as they are the ones at the bedside and thus able to see the issues that have the greatest affect on patient care.

 

The Customer Service group was empowered as a decision-making group. Once an issue was identified, an owner would be designated and it was expected that the issue would be resolved. The owner of the outstanding issue would be responsible for reporting on progress toward resolving it at the next weekly meeting. 

 

June 2015
The first Customer Service meeting was held on June 2, 2015 with more than 35 leaders, clinical nurses and other members in attendance. Clinical nurses were asked about what was interfering in providing excellent nursing care. With the input of the clinical nurses, the team identified and prioritized the most important items and identified solutions to be implemented.

 

Issue

Proposed Solution

Physicians not calling staff back in a timely manner. 

Donna Hoban, MD, Senior Vice President and Physician in Chief, would address the issue with the individual physician.

Patient not knowing about new medications.

With the input of clinical nurses, it was decided that a pharmacist would round on all patients with newly prescribed pain medications to address pain, medication indications and side effects, and evaluate whether there are other interventions that could help with pain.

Environment Issues – Clinical nurses expressed concerns about the noise and cleanliness on the units.

Based on clinical nurses’ concerns, the following decisions were made to improve the environment:          

  • A quiet kit was developed including the following items: ear plugs, eye mask, lip balm and do not disturb sign for the door from Inventory Control.
  • All noisy equipment and carts were repaired by Facilities Management.
  • Lights on the nursing units were dimmed by Facilities Management from 8:30 p.m. to 6 a.m.

 

These are only some of the decisions that were made based on the contribution of the clinical nurses in the Customer Service meeting. 

 

July-August 2015
The weekly meeting continued, and clinical nurses worked with other members of the team to identify ways to improve the care of the patients at BGP.

Outcomes
Clinical nurses’ contributions as members of the organization-level decision-making group in the Customer Service meetings improved care processes for patients and the patients’ overall perceptions of the hospital. BGP’s Overall Rating increased from the 46th percentile in March 2015 to the 76th percentile in September 2015, 92nd in October 2015, 76th in November 2015 and 83rd in December 2015.

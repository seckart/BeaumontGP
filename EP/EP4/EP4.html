EP4

 

Care Delivery System(s) EP4

 

Nurses create partnerships with patients and families to establish goals and plans for delivery of patient-centered care.

  • Provide one example, with supporting evidence, of nurses partnering with patients and families to develop an individualized plan of care based on the unique needs of the patient.
    AND
  • Provide one example, with supporting evidence, of nurses partnering with patients and families to improve systems of care at the unit, service line or organizational level.

 

 

Example One: Individualized Plan of Care Based on the Unique Needs of a Patient

 

Good outcomes are not always easy to achieve. For patients with compromised physical, mental, emotional and environmental needs, the nurse must partner with the patient, family and care team to create an individualized Plan of Care to meet the patient’s complex needs. 

 

One example of nurses partnering with a patient with unique needs began in August 2015 when A.H. made her first visit to the Beaumont Grosse Pointe (BGP) Emergency Center (EC) to receive care. A.H. is a 19-year-old woman with chronic type I diabetes and myriad other health issues that require her to keep a strict medication and diet regimen.

 

Since August 2015, A.H. has had 43 admissions to BGP with lengths of stay ranging from two to seven days. She typically presents herself to the EC in a crisis seeking admission.

EP4, Diagnoses of A.H.

 

A.H. was first admitted to BGP on August 8, 2015 with a diagnosis of diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA). She was admitted to the Critical Care Unit (CCU) for treatment and was discharged on August 10, 2015, only to return to the EC on August 12, 2015 and be readmitted to CCU with the same diagnosis.

 

Her admissions continued monthly, and sometimes more frequently, for more than a year. The most common diagnosis was abdominal pain secondary to DKA. Non-compliant diabetic activity opens the patient up to many different comorbidities. (Evidence EP4-1, Screen Shots of A.H. Admission)

 

Based on the many diagnoses and admissions of this young patient, A.H. has many unique needs to be addressed, including:

  • Education on the disease process
  • Individualized plan to prevent hospital admission
  • Individualized home care services

 

Partnering with the Patient and Family
One example of a partnership with the patient comes from November 4, 2016, when Holly Cory, RN, CCM, Nurse Care Manager, met with A.H. to discuss her visit to a new physician and the possibility of getting an insulin pump that her insurance would cover.  A.H. explained to Cory that she believed the visits were helping her understand and cope with her diabetes. Cory partnered with A.H. to obtain the equipment that was required to ensure she was receiving the proper care. The plan was to find an insulin pump that her insurance would cover. A.H. informed Cory that, as part of her Plan of Care, she would continue to follow up with the new physician every Wednesday until Thanksgiving.

 

On November 10, 2016, during one of her frequent admissions, a multidisciplinary meeting was led by Mark Alrais, MD, the patient’s primary provider. The purpose of the meeting was to partner with A.H. and her family to develop an individualized care plan for A.H. moving forward. The following care team members were present:

  • A.H., the patient
  • Cherie Cusimano, BSN, RN, clinical nurse, 3 West
  • Holly Cory, RN, CCM, Care Manager
  • Christie Hoffman, RN-BC, Charge Nurse, 3 West
  • Patricia Ackley, BSN, RN, Administrative Manager, 3 West
  • Leanna Beattie, LMSW, Social Worker
  • Michael Brennan, DO, Consultant Physician
  • Mark Alrais, MD, Primary Care Physician
  • The patient’s mother

 

Alrais reviewed the status of A.H.’s medical condition with all present. He explained the seriousness of her disease, why what she was doing was not improving her health and the importance of following her Plan of Care. A.H. agreed to the Plan of Care that was discussed: to better adhere to the medical regimen, try to avoid behaviors that were preventing a better outcome and follow up on recommendations. 

 

On April 5, 2017, Cory continued to partner with A.H. to discuss post-discharge care.  Cory explained to A.H. the importance of follow-up care post-hospitalization, stressing the importance of answering the phone when the home care nurse was calling. Cory explained that A.H. needed to be responsible for her care, and A.H. agreed that she would work with the home care team.

 

On April 6, 2017, Cory again partnered with A.H. regarding her home care, as based on their conversation from April 5 she was not confident that A.H. would follow up with home care. During the conservation on April 5, Cory had explained to A.H. that she needed to answer the phone when the home care nurse called to make an appointment for her to receive the IV therapy. A.H. agreed that she would comply with Cory’s instruction.

 

Cory suggested that A.H. go to an infusion center to receive the prescribed antibiotic treatment to assist with her recovery. Cory told A.H. that she would arrange transportation from her insurance carrier to get her to the infusion center: They would pick her up at her home, take her to the infusion center and return her to her home after the treatment. After careful consideration, A.H. requested that she receive the treatment in her home rather than going to the infusion center. Cory agreed to continue to support A.H. with her expressed preference.

 

During A.H.s’ June 14, 2017 hospital visit, Jacquelyn Bowness, BSN, RN, clinical nurse, 3 West, explained to the patient the importance of following her diet when A.H. wanted to order pizza from a local restaurant. To help A.H. adhere to her prescribed Plan of Care, Bowness partnered with A.H. to discuss her food choice and help her understand the need to make better decisions about her diet. After this discussion, A.H. agreed that she would not order food from the outside and would obtain meals only from the dietary department. (Evidence EP4-2, Electronic Medical Record from Multidisciplinary Meeting and Screen Shot of Electronic Medical Records)   

 

Individualized Care for the Patient
It was apparent that A.H. required an individualized Plan of Care to manage her multiple diagnoses and care requirements. Examples of individualized care include:

  • Admit to consistent unit for all admissions
  • Consistent Nurse Care Manager
  • Education and individualized care

 

Admit to Consistent Unit
During one of A.H.s’ admissions in March 2016, 3 West clinical nurses approached Patricia Ackley, BSN, RN, Administrative Manager, 3 West, regarding the chronic lack of outpatient follow-up in the care of A.H. They suggested that an ethics consult be ordered to assist with A.H.’s care and that an individualized Plan of Care be developed.

 

An ethics consult was completed on November 3, 2016 with Edward Neumaier, BSN, CHPCN, Palliative Care Coordinator. After meeting with the patient, Neumaier met with the clinical nurse and Ackley to assist in developing a mutually agreed upon Plan of Care with follow-up. Neumaier believed that providing consistent care to A.H. would assist in following up on the Plan of Care, and that having the same clinical nurses and Nurse Care Manager care for A.H. would build relationships and help her partner with the clinical staff to follow her Plan of Care. (Evidence EP4-3, Ethics Consult) After the intake note was completed, the ethics consult was cancelled because the physician was going to have a family meeting with the patient, her mother and the clinical staff.

 

If A.H. were to require a higher level of care, the admitting department was aware that she should return to her home unit when medical/surgical care was warranted.

 

Consistent Nurse Care Manager
In November 2016,a consistent Nurse Care Manager was assigned to A.H. to provide individualized care. It was believed that this arrangement would assist in providing consistent care to the patient.

 

In November 2016,Cory spoke about this case with Deborah Klein, LMSW, ACUS, Director of Care Management, and they decided to assign the patient to the same Nurse Care Manager and Social Worker for continuity of care. Cory spoke with A.H.’s long-term case manager at Molina Health to advise her of the Plan of Care. Klein also spoke with the home care clinical nurse to gain insight into the patient’s home situation from a nursing perspective. (Evidence EP4-4, Electronic Medical Record for Cory)

 

Once this plan was established, the care team was quick to review the Plan of Care and ensure that consults were placed appropriately and in a timely manner. Care Management and the Social Worker were notified of the patient’s admission. Clinical nurses and the Care Manager conducted progressive rounds daily to reestablish the patient's individualized Plan of Care. 

 

This individualized Plan of Care to assign A.H. to one nursing unit and one Nurse Care Manager helped clinical nurses and ancillary services provide the patient and family with the best care possible. This partnership between nurses and the patient would develop a relationship with the clinical nurses and Nurse Care Manager, and the staff would also develop a relationship with the patient. The patient said this was a great idea.

 

Education and Individualized Plan of Care
Based on the family meeting on November 10, 2016, a detailed individualized Plan of Care was established. The following were agreed to by the patient, family and care team:

  • Importance of compliance with medications and diet.
  • Plans for follow-up at the Gastrointestinal Center at Beaumont Royal Oak for more advanced therapies.
  • An insulin pump was discussed, and application paperwork for an insulin pump was given to the patient.

 

Other examples of individualized care include clinical nurses providing education on the insulin pump on February 19, 2017. The clinical nurses identified that A.H. was having multiple issues using the pump.

 

On May 8, 2017, Susan Notoriano, RN, clinical nurse, 3 West, believed that A.H. could use help with relaxation. A. H. agreed with her recommendation, so Notoriano taught the patient relaxation techniques and provided individualized care addressing exercise, carbohydrates and the effect of insulin.

 

Since the multidisciplinary meeting in November 2016, A.H. has been admitted to BGP 13 times, each time continuing with the individualized Plan of Care that had been outlined earlier. A.H. was admitted to 3 West and had the same Nurse Care Manager. The clinical nurse continued to provide education and reinforce the education on A.H.’s diabetes and kidney issues.(Evidence EP4-5, Electronic Medical Records Notes and Care Plan Notes for A.H.)

 

Although A.H. remains a challenging patient with unique needs, the clinical nurses of BGP continue to partner with her to help her manage her many care needs.

 

Example Two: Partnering with a Patient and Family to Improve the Care in the Emergency Center

 

Care Provided in the Emergency Center
On June 26, 2016, A.Z. arrived at the Beaumont Grosse Pointe (BGP) Emergency Center (EC) verbalizing abdominal cramping and vaginal bleeding. This patient was 16 weeks pregnant upon arrival. After the initial pelvic exam performed by an EC mid-level provider, A.Z. was ordered an ultrasound.

 

During the ultrasound, the fetus passed out of the uterus and into the vaginal canal. Subsequent ultrasounds indicated an empty uterus with irregular endometrium. The ultrasound technician reported to the EC that the fetus had passed out of the uterus. Upon returning to the EC, the patient was placed in a private room and the fetus was removed from the vagina by the physician assistant.

 

On June 27, 2016, the patient’s mother called the hospital to express concerns about her daughter’s care, saying that the clinical nurses and physician assistant had “no empathy toward her daughter.” She further explained that she was not happy that her daughter had waited approximately 30 minutes before a medical exam was performed. The patient’s mother was also not satisfied with the lack of emotional support for her daughter following the perinatal loss.

 

Steve Apigo, BSN, RN, Director of Emergency Center, was notified of the mother’s concern regarding the care. Apigo then notified Glen Clark, MD, Chief, Emergency Center; Maureen Bennett, Service Excellence; and Anne Stewart, MBA, BSN, RN, NE-BC, Vice President and Chief Nursing Officer. Bennett suggested that a stakeholders meeting be held to review the care.

EP4, Care Improvement Participants

 

The participants reviewed the case, and Clark determined that the medical care and the care in the ultrasound department had been appropriate. Apigo reviewed the electronic medical record and had a discussion with the EC clinical nurses who had been involved in the patient’s care. (Evidence EP4-6, Timeline of Care in EC)

 

After reviewing the medical record and discussing the case with the clinical nurses, it was determined that the hospital’s policy was followed in the care of the patient and of the fetus. However, elements of the process involving the emotional care of the patient had been omitted. The following aspects of hospital policy had not been followed:

  • Wrap fetus in blanket
  • Notify OB
  • Notify Spiritual Services
  • Clean infant; allow patients to assist
  • Give family opportunity to hold fetus
  • Give family opportunity to name baby
  • Take pictures of fetus with parents’ consent
  • Prepare mementos if possible 
  • Give all photos/mementos to parents prior to discharge: do not store any copies

(Evidence EP4-7, Hospital Policy P-4 Perinatal Loss, Care of Fetus/Infant)

 

When the patient was discharged, she and her mother told Marion that they would like to arrange to pick up the fetus so they could have a funeral.

 

On June 27, 2016, Marion met with the Pathology department and prepared for the family to pick up the fetus. After making the necessary arrangements, Marion notified the family that they could pick up the fetus, and a funeral was planned by the family.

 

Partnering with the Patient and Family to Improve the System of Care
Bennett contacted the family to express condolences on behalf of BGP and offer them the opportunity to come to the hospital to share their concerns with hospital staff members and identify ways to improve the care in the Emergency Center. The patient and family agreed to partner with Emergency Center nursing on ways to improve care.  (Evidence EP4-8, Appointment with Hospital, Patient and Family)

 

On July 26, 2016, Swaine facilitated a meeting involving nurses, a physician, and the patient and family to improve the system of care in the EC for patients and families experiencing fetal demise. At this meeting, the patient’s mother reiterated her displeasure with how her daughter’s emotional needs were not met during her care in the EC. The patient also expressed concerns about the care and how the fetus was referred to as a “specimen” instead of a baby. The mother believed there was a lack of empathy toward her daughter, who agreed. 

 

The patient also said that she was not given an opportunity to grieve over the loss of her baby, and that patients experiencing this type of loss should have an opportunity to express their feelings. Her intention was to advise the nurse so this does not happen to another patient.    

 

The patient said she would like to see the clinical nurses in the Emergency Center better prepared to provide care for patients with the diagnosis of fetal demise. Specifically, the patient said it would have been helpful if clinical nurses were educated on terminology not to use, such as “specimen,” when talking about the fetus. The patient and her mother also suggested the EC take into consideration the emotions of families experiencing perinatal loss, and that additional resources be provided for the family.

 

Apigo agreed with the patient and her family that these actionable items were appropriate, and he said that a legacy plan would be put in place for new clinical nurses hired into the EC. The family was agreeable to this plan and was informed that an action plan would be implemented based on their input and that they would be updated on its progress. 

 

Following the meeting with the family, Apigo; Marion; Bradley O’Shell, BSN, RN, Assistant Nurse Manager, EC; and Sandra Rizzo, MSN, RN, CEN, Clinical Nurse Specialist, EC, began education for clinical nurses about the care of patients presenting to the EC with the diagnosis of fetal demise.

 

The nurses indicated that they felt emotionally overwhelmed by the event, but that many were not aware of every detail of the hospital’s policy. EC leaders met to create an action plan based on the partnership with the patient and her family to provide education and prepare bedside caregivers for these types of event.

 

Rizzo was responsible for developing and providing the education to the clinical nurses, which was done one-on-one and in small groups. Handouts were developed to ensure the staff was providing consistent quality care to patients who experience a perinatal loss. The handouts were new to the staff, who agreed they were a good idea so they would know what is expected when a patient with a perinatal loss presents to the EC.  The following were developed to assist the staff:

  • Perinatal Loss Checklist & Follow-up
  • Perinatal Loss Identification
  • Preparing Baby/Fetus for Final Disposition

 

Improvement in the Care
As an outcome of partnering with the patient and her family in response to the care she had received in the EC, Rizzo met with Marisa Engel, MSN, RNC-OB, C-EFM, Clinical Nurse Specialist, Family Birth Center, to discuss ways to improve the care for maternity care patients in the EC. After reviewing the incident with Engels, Rizzo implemented the following to improve the system of care:

  • Clinical nurses were provided education as a group and individually regarding the care of patients with fetal demise.
  • Clinical nurses were provided education about how to provide emotional support for both the patient and family.
  • An education table was initiated in the Emergency Center, displaying educational materials and handouts on fetal demise for clinical nurses to review.
  • The Fetal Demise Educational Resource Binder was updated and made available for all to access as a reference.
  • Spiritual Care to be notified of all patients admitted with fetal demise.
  • Social Worker to be notified of all patients admitted with fetal demise.
  • The Perinatal Loss Program at Beaumont Royal Oak was investigated for possible implementation at BGP.
  • A plan was developed to initiate “The Pause” in the Emergency Center.  

 

“The Pause” is a moment of silence after a patient's death to honor the patient after they pass away. In this case, it would involve fetal demise. A multicultural group in the same room focuses on one point, the patient who has passed away. They honor the patient as a group, but doing it in silence as individuals in their own personalized way, whether in prayer, in thinking positive thoughts or just recognizing the moment and its importance. This process is a nurse-driven initiative in collaboration with the patient and family.

 

It was important that that the clinical nurses understood the hospital policy regarding fetal demise to ensure that the following care is provided to the patient and the family. 

  • Wrap fetus in blanket
  • Clean infant; allow patients to assist
  • Give family opportunity to hold fetus
  • Give family opportunity to name baby

 

Partnering with the patient and family enabled the EC to see what is important to the patient and develop strategies to ensure the care is improved for patients.

 

In addition to the education the clinical nurses received regarding fetal demise, the improvements in the care of patients with fetal demise were instituted. The following practice changes were made in the EC to improve the care of patients:

  • Follow the Perinatal Loss, Care of Fetus/Infant P-4 policy from the Family Birth Center in the Emergency Center
  • Distribute the “Miscarriage” or “Ectopic Pregnancy” brochures to the patient
  • Consult to Spiritual Care
  • Review Disposition and Final Arrangements (Private Funeral Home, A Child Remembered Program individual niche or A Child Remembered Program Community Internment)
  • Complete Grief Support Referral Form as necessary

(Evidence EP4-9, Checklist for Perinatal Loss, Policy P-4 Perinatal Loss, Care of Fetus/Infant, Brochures and Grief Support Referral)

 

Follow-up Care for Patient
A follow-up letter was sent to the patient on July 27, 2016, updating her on how the hospital used her suggestions and her collaboration with the clinical nurses to improve care in the Emergency Center. In the letter she was also thanked for her honest and open feedback about her care, which led to a partnership with the patient and family to improve care in the Emergency Center. (Evidence EP4-10, Follow-up Letter to Patient)

 

After partnering with the patient and family and determining the course of action to improve care at the system level in the EC, there have been no further issues with patients who present in the EC for care with the diagnosis of fetal demise.

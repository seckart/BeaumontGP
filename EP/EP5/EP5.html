EP5

 

Care Delivery System(s) EP5

 

Nurses are involved in interprofessional collaborative practice within the care delivery system to ensure care coordination and continuity of care.

  • Provide two examples, with supporting evidence, of nurses' involvement in interprofessional collaborative practice that ensures care coordination and continuity of patient care.

 

 

Example One: Nurse Navigator for the Congestive Heart Failure and Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease Populations

 

Beaumont Grosse Pointe (BGP) offers a disease-specific Nurse Navigator to assist patients and their families with understanding and managing the disease process. The Navigator provides consistent education to congestive heart failure (CHF) and chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) patients. Education may include:

  • Disease management
  • Nutrition information
  • Post-discharge follow-up 

 

The Nurse Navigator follows patients from admission to post-discharge to continue and reinforce teaching efforts and to ensure care coordination and continuity of patient care after discharge from BGP.

 

In her role as Nurse Navigator, Johanna Castronero, BSN, RN provides consistent care coordination throughout the continuum of care for CHF and COPD patients, their families and caregivers. Castronero helps patients navigate the healthcare system to ensure a smooth transition from setting to setting, and she educates patients and their families on disease process and management of CHF and COPD. She is responsible for communicating and collaborating with the interprofessional team to manage patient care across the continuum. (Evidence EP5-1, Job Description of Nurse Navigator)

 

CHF and COPD patients are given a direct phone line to the Nurse Navigator so they and their families can contact her with any questions they have while in the hospital or after discharge. Castronero provides patient and family-centered education based on her assessment of the patients’ needs. 

 

Interprofessional Collaborative Practice and Care Coordination Across the Continuum
Castronero’s work with patient J.A. provides one example of her collaborative practice and care coordination. JA is a 66-year-old male with multiple healthcare issues; his main diagnoses are CHF, cardiomyopathy, hypertension, diabetes and coronary artery disease. 

 

During J.A.’s June 2017 admission to BGP, Castronero spoke with Georges Ghafari, MD, Chief of Cardiology, and Cynthia Piko, MD, Internal Medicine, regarding the case.  Castronero reviewed with Ghafari the patient’s history, which included multiple admissions with CHF over a short period of time. Ghafari suggested that Castronero coordinate an appointment for J.A. at the University of Michigan (U of M) to see someone in the heart failure clinic, as Ghafari believed a second opinion was warranted for this gentleman.

 

Castronero called the University of Michigan Heart Failure and Transplant clinic and spoke with Mary, an RN at the U of M CHF clinic. Castronero reviewed the patient history with Mary and an appointment with a cardiologist was made for June 19, 2017 at 12:40 p.m. To assist with the coordination of care, Castronero coordinated the transfer of the following medical records:

  • All DC summaries
  • Cardiac testing
  • Laboratory results
  • Medication list
    (Evidence EP5-2, Castronero Worksheet for JA)

 

On June 14, 2017, Castronero collaborated with J.A., Ghafari and Lesja Bontomsi, BSN, RN, clinical nurse, Critical Care Stepdown Unit, to review the plan of care for J.A. and coordinate the consult at the University of Michigan. (Evidence EP5-3, Screen Shot from Electronic Medical Record, June 14, 2017)

 

On June 20, 2017, Castronero received a fax from Nicklas at the U of M Heart Failure and Transplant Clinic regarding the consult with the patient to assist Castronero, Ghafari and Piko in the continued management of the patient’s care at Beaumont Grosse Pointe. (Evidence EP5-4, Follow-up Consult from University of Michigan) 

 

On June 20 and 21, 2017, Castronero continued the coordination of care across the continuum by making a discharge follow-up call to J.A. and by speaking with Sandy at the U of M to verify that the patient had made a follow-up appointment with Nicklas there. 

 

Castronero spoke with J.A. on June 20, 2017, and he said that he was not going to follow up with Nicklas at the U of M and that he was instead going to see Ghafari and Quresh Khairullah, MD, Director Nephrology, Beaumont Grosse Pointe. Castronero then spoke with J.A. on June 21, 2017 to give him information about the follow-up visits with Ghafari and Kaharullah. (Evidence EP5-5, Screen Shots from Electronic Medical Record dated June 20, 2017 and June 21, 2017)

 

The Navigator’s role is to continue the coordination of care with other members of the care team to formulate optimal discharge plans for patients. A post-discharge call is therefore made to COPD and CHF patients who agree to this, which allows for the reassessment of the patient’s understanding of the education provided. It also gives the Navigator an opportunity to reinforce teaching, answer questions and provide follow-up support/problem solving.

 

Example Two: Nurse Care Managers Collaborate and Coordinate the Care of Patients
Beaumont Grosse Pointe (BGP) adopted a formal discharge planning process in early 2015 as an initiative to reduce readmissions.Discharge planning begins upon admission when patients are screened for discharge needs and the patient and family are given education to prepare them for their journey after discharge.

 

The care coordinators at BGP are referred to as Nurse Care Managers (NCM). The purpose of this role is the daily organization and facilitation of caseloads in the Emergency Center or the unit where they are assigned. The NCMs review and evaluate the appropriateness of admission or continued stay based on defined criteria.

 

The main responsibilities of the NCMs include:

  • Facilitating care during stay, and planning and expediting plans for safe and effective discharge. 
  • Coordinating care across the continuum by considering all patient needs
  • Collaborating with the Utilization Resource Center to obtain authorization for care and maximize reimbursement
    (Evidence EP5-6, Job Description of Care Coordinator/Care Manager)

Interprofessional Collaborative Practice and Care Coordination
NCMs practice on all units at BGP. 3 West is a busy 45-bed medical/surgical unit with a specialty of gastrointestinal surgical patients. To assist in the coordination of care, a morning huddle is held at 10 a.m. to facilitate smooth communication between clinical nurses and NCMs.

 

Each clinical nurse reviews their patient assignment and updates the NCM on the nursing care plan and the concerns of the patient and family. The NCM relays to the clinical nurse any issues or concerns in the discharge planning process.

 

The case of B.V., a 61-year-old female, provides an example of an NCM collaborating and coordinating care. B.V. was admitted on April 29, 2017 after presenting to the BGP Emergency Center with a diagnosis of abdominal pain. Her extensive medical and surgical history includes:

  • Depression
  • Obesity
  • Asthma
  • Hernia
  • Appendectomy
  • Cholecystectomy
  • Hernia repair

 

The initial care management assessment of discharge needs was completed on May 1, 2017, and NCM Sarah Griffith, BSN, RN, Nursing Care Manager, Care Management, identified no needs at the time and the plan was for B.V. to return home. However, as her health quickly changed the NCMs and various professionals providing care began their collaboration and coordination of care. 

 

On May 9, 2017, the patient’s care needs had changed and at that point the plan of care was unclear. Victoria Robinson, BSN, RN, Nurse Care Manager, Care Management, collaborated with Larry Lloyd, MD, physician, to plan for B.V.’s needs. Robinson coordinated the plans to allow for home TPN.

. 
Robinson also collaborated with Leanna Beattie, LMSW, Social Worker, on her recommendation about whether the patient would be able to return home or should be admitted to a sub-acute rehabilitation facility (SAR). (Evidence EP5-7, Screen Shots from Electronic Medical Record dated May 1, May 9 and May 11, 2017)

 

On May 15, 2017, B.V.’s care needs changed again and she was transferred to the Critical Care Unit. Craig Eckenrode, BSN, RN, Nurse Care Manager, Care Management, assumed care for B.V. The plan for B.V. at this time was for her to be admitted to an SAR. A BGP Nurse Care Manager was assigned to continue to follow B.V. in this new setting. Eckenrode noted that physical and occupational therapy should be ordered once B.V. was medically stable. (Evidence EP5-8, Screen Shots from Electronic Medical Record dated May 15 and May 19, 2017)

 

On June 6, 2017, B.V. was transferred to 3 West, a medical/surgical unit at BGP, where Holly Cory, RN, CCM, Nurse Care Manager, Care Management, assumed care for B.V.  Cory collaborated with Lloyd on the plan of care, and Lloyd informed Cory that the patient would require long-term care. Cory collaborated with social worker Beattie to determine the proper placement for the patient to ensure that her care was coordinated and seamless.  

 

To ensure the smooth transfer of care from BGP to either an SAR or long-term acute care hospital, Cory participated in a family meeting on June 9, 2017 that included the patient, her family, family practice residents, a physician, a social worker, a nurse and a palliative care nurse. This interprofessional meeting was held to discuss the status of the patient, answer the family’s questions and coordinate care to meet the patient and family’s goals for care.

 

During the family meeting, it was decided that a consult to Palliative Care would be helpful. The consult was completed on June 9, 2017 by Joyce Kerdziel, BSN, RN, Palliative Care Nurse. (Evidence EP5-9, Screen Shots from Electronic Medical Record dated June 6 and June 9, 2017 and Palliative Care Consult)

 

On June 21, 2017, Brenda Davis, BSN, RN, Hospice Nurse, met with the patient, her sister and brother, Beattie and Cory to discuss the plan of care. After much discussion, collaboration and coordination, it was decided based on the patient’s care needs that she would go to an extended care facility. (Evidence EP5-10, Screen Shot Electronic Medical Record, Hospice Note of June 21, 2017)

 

To ensure a smooth transition from the hospital to home and to provide seamless care, the NCM is responsible for ensuring that the patient and family are ready for discharge.  They are responsible for ordering equipment, beds and supplies (IV meds, tube feedings, wound vacs and walkers) to ensure the family has all the appropriate resources to care for the patient when they return to their home setting. 

 

It is also the NCMs’ responsibility to verify benefits for medications, rehab services and home care. They also set up discharge transportation, referrals to Social Work for extended care facility placement and community resources such as Meals on Wheels. It is the NCMs’ responsibility to ensure the patient and family have all the equipment needed to provide the required care in the home.

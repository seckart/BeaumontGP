Accountability, Competence, and Autonomy EP16

 

Nurse autonomy is supported and promoted through the organization's governance structure for shared decision-making.

  • Provide one example, with supporting evidence, of clinical autonomy that demonstrates the authority and freedom of nurses to make nursing care decisions (within the full scope of their practice) in the clinical care of patients.
    AND

  • Provide one example, with supporting evidence, of organizational autonomy that demonstrates the authority and freedom of nurses' involvement in broader unit, service line, organization, or system decision-making processes pertaining to patient care, policies and procedures, or work environment.

 

 

 

Example One: Use of Extended Dwell Catheters versus Central Line Catheters

 

Patients with central lines are at an increased risk for central line-associated bloodstream infections (CLABSI), which are dangerous for the patient and costly to the healthcare organization. According to the Agency for Healthcare Research and Quality (AHRQ), the average cost of a CLABSI is $45,254 and the additional average length of stay for a CLABSI patient is 13 days. The risk for bloodstream infection rises each time a central line is accessed for the administration of fluids, medications and laboratory blood draws.

 

CLABSI is a patient quality core measure that is reported to NDNQI and insurance companies and used as a basis for reimbursement. The goal for all hospitals is zero CLABSIs, and this is considered best practice. Beaumont Grosse Pointe (BGP) had a CLABSI rate of 1.96 incidents per 1,000 central line days in first quarter 2015, with a decrease to 1.50 in second quarter 2015.

 

In March 2015, to positively impact this measure and improve the quality of patient care, BGP had to change the practice of inserting central lines when not necessary. Janet Hamlin, MA, BSN, RN, Administrative Manager, Internal Resource Team and Nursing Business Office, reviewed the literature and noted that the use of extended dwell peripheral intravenous catheters decreased CLABSI rates and were being used as an alternative to central lines.

 

Hamlin reviewed an article, “Accepted but Unacceptable: Peripheral IV Catheter Failure” in the journal The Art and Science of Nursing, and spoke with Julie Giordano, BSN, RN, Manager, Vascular Access Team, Beaumont Farmington Hills, about the use of extended dwell (midline) catheters. Hamlin also discussed the use of extended dwell catheters with Nicholas Gilpin, DO, Medical Director, Infection Control and Epidemiology, Beaumont Grosse Pointe. (Evidence EP16-1, Email, Article and Policy of Beaumont Farmington Hills)

 

In May 2015, Hamlin and the IV Team created a policy on the use of extended dwell catheters for Beaumont Grosse Point. (Evidence EP16-2, Extended Dwell Peripheral Intravenous Catheter) Prior to this, BGP did not have an alternative to central lines. This policy defined the proper insertion guidelines, care and maintenance of midlines and the removal of midlines.

 

The scope of the policy is for nurses responsible for the insertion, care and maintenance of the midlines in the inpatient and Emergency Center settings. The practice of inserting extended dwell catheters is an alternative to central line placement that is available in clinical nurses’ practice. These are to be inserted by qualified personnel who have received appropriate training, including the IV Team and midlevel providers. (Evidence EP16-3, Request to Develop Report for Midline Catheter and Add to LDA Report)

 

In May 2015, a Bard representative provided education to the IV Team clinical nurses so they would be proficient in inserting midlines. This education for the IV Team nurses was in accord with Standard #6 Competence and Competency Validation, of the Infusion Nurses Society Guidelines, which state:

  • As a method of public protection, the nurse shall be competent in the safe delivery of infusion therapy within her or his scope of practice
  • The nurse shall be responsible and accountable for attaining and maintaining competence with infusion therapy within her or his scope of practice
  • Competency Validation shall be performed initially and on an ongoing basis
  • Competency Validation shall be documented in accordance with organizational policies, procedures and practice guidelines.

 

The education was provided to the IV Team consisting of clinical nurses Casmira Gualtieri, RN; Linda Plunkett, RN, VA-BC; Cindy Morris, BSN, RN; and Carolyn Ward, BSN, RN. The practice of inserting midlines then became available in the clinical nurses’ practice when considering an alternative to central line placement. 

 

In June 2015, the IV Team clinical nurses began inserting extended dwell catheters. After receiving the education, they had the clinical autonomy to assess the need for and to insert midlines.

 

Autonomy in Decision Making
When an inpatient nurse pages the IV Team to assess the patient’s need for an extended dwell catheter rather than a central line, the IV clinical nurse makes an independent decision about which to use. The decision to use the extended dwell catheter or central line  is based on the IV Team nurse’s assessment of the patient. The nurse assesses the:

  • Adequacy of the patient’s vasculature
  • Medications the patient is receiving
  • Diagnostic testing that has been ordered
  • Patient’s intended length of stay

(Evidence EP16-4, Job Description for IV Nurse)

 

The midline is typically for short-term use, less than 29 days of medication. Blood can be drawn from midlines, and they are acceptable for use with power injectors. Power injectors are used during diagnostic exams, such as a CT scan in Radiology, when dye is injected into the patient.

 

The IV nurse has the autonomy to determine, based on her or his assessment, whether the patient is a candidate for the midline. If not, the practice is for the IV nurse to make a referral to have a peripherally inserted central catheter (PICC) placed in Interventional Radiology.

 

Nursing Care Decision Regarding IV Placement
On March 24, 2017, A.R., a 91-year-old female, was admitted to the Critical Care Stepdown Unit with a diagnosis of mental status changes and UTI. Within the first couple of days, she had two peripheral IVs that infiltrated and had multiple venipunctures for lab draws. On March 27, 2017, the clinical nurse caring for A.R. paged IV nurse Gualtieri to assess the patient for a midline.

 

Gualtieri reviewed the patient’s chart, noting the medication orders and the order for a CT. It appeared that the patient would be admitted for another couple of days because she was on antibiotic therapy. After analyzing this information, Gualtieri performed a thorough physical examination of A.R.’s extremities, noting her frail, thin skin that tore easily. As her mental status led her to get agitated easily, she was not an ideal patient to undergo multiple lab draw attempts.

 

Gualtieri made an informed nursing care decision to insert a midline rather than trying another peripheral that may go sub-Q during the CT. Another advantage of the midline was that clinical nurses would be able to draw labs from it, relieving A.R. of the trauma of multiple venipunctures.

 

On March 30, 2017, based on the clinical information that the patient was going to be discharged and receiving IV antibiotics at home, IV nurse Morris made an independent decision to call Interventional Radiology and have a PICC placed prior to discharge. (Evidence EP16-5, Screen Shot from Electronic Medical Record; Inserting Midline, Documentation of Midline and Referral to IR for PICC Placement) 

 

On March 31, IV nurse Morris was paged to assess A.R.’s midline site prior to discharge. While trying to discontinue the midline prior to discharge, the clinical nurse caring for A.R. met some resistance. The clinical nurse paged Morris, who was able to assist the patient without causing her discomfort. Morris instructed the patient and family to call the doctor if they noted bleeding or drainage at the site or if she had pain or tenderness.

 

Clinical nurses on the units appreciate being able to contact an IV clinical nurse for an assessment of whether a midline can be placed. This is less time consuming for the clinical nurse than prepping and transporting a patient off the unit for a central line, and it allows for more attentive care, less risk of CLABSIs and better quality and safety outcomes.

 

 

Example Two:Organizational Autonomy – Nurses Lead and Participate in the Local and Organizational Structures and Processes that Support the Identification and Treatment of Septic Patients

 

Sepsis is a life-threatening condition with a high mortality rate, and early identification and treatment is crucial to improving patient outcomes. Building processes for screening and identifying these patients requires interprofessional planning and teamwork.

 

The key roles that nurses play in treating septic patients include early recognition and early goal-directed therapy. The septic patient’s outcome is improved with early recognition of the signs and symptoms of sepsis and the timely initiation of antibiotics and fluid resuscitation. Nurses are instrumental in the successful coordination of care that is imperative for positive outcomes. Nurses are responsible for care delivery at every phase and are integral to the planning, development and implementation of the process.

 

Two interprofessional committees, the Corporate Sepsis Workgroup and the Beaumont Grosse Pointe Sepsis Team, have been influential in developing sepsis care processes and for continuing assessment of the care provided to sepsis patients.

 

Corporate Sepsis Workgroup
The Corporate Sepsis Workgroup, chaired by William Anderson, MD, includes representatives from Medical/Surgical nursing, Critical Care nursing, Emergency Department, Information Technology and physicians from five of the eight Beaumont Health hospitals. Its purpose is to address many of the system issues surrounding sepsis care and to ensure that clinical practice is consistent with the best practice requirements for meeting the sepsis Core Measures. The key to improving sepsis mortality is faster detection.

 

The key strategies of this workgroup were to:

  • Develop a means to identify the potentially septic patient and alert the clinical nurse
  • Provide autonomy for the clinical nurse to activate the Rapid Response Team (RRT) based on clinical judgements
  • Collaborate with the RRT and house physician to ensure the patient receives early goal-directed therapy
  • Improve compliance with sepsis Core Measures
  • Review and analyze data on sepsis cases to focus on early recognition and treatment of sepsis

EP16, Corporate Sepsis Workgroup

 

One key area of clinical practice that was developed by the interprofessional Corporate Sepsis Workgroup was the Sepsis Surveillance Alert. This automatic alert is generated in the electronic medical record (EMR) to notify the clinical nurse that a patient is exhibiting signs and symptoms of sepsis.

 

In February 2015, the Corporate Sepsis Workgroup defined the criteria associated with sepsis, and the Information Technology (IT) department created an algorithm in the EMR that checks all patients for these criteria. When a patient meets the criteria for possible sepsis, an alert fires to notify the clinical nurse. The clinical nurse then clinically and critically assesses the patient and autonomously decides whether the RRT or house physician needs to be called. (Evidence EP16-6, Sepsis Committee Workgroup Minutes, February 19, 2015)

 

In March 2015, a pilot of the best practice alert (BPA) began on two of BGP’s busiest medical/surgical units, 2 Southeast and 3 West, and its progressive care unit, the Critical Care Stepdown Unit. (Evidence EP16-7, IT Communication Sepsis Surveillance Alert Pilot Unit Expansion) The trial was designed to test the criteria selected to identify septic patients. During the pilot, the Corporate Sepsis Workgroup reviewed all cases in which the BPA fired to determine its sensitivity. The group discovered that the BPA was too sensitive and was firing for many patients who were not septic.

 

The Corporate Sepsis Workgroup discussed the sepsis alert pilot at its March 30, 2016 meeting. Based on the pilot and feedback from the clinical nurses, the workgroup reviewed and revised the criteria, narrowed them down to eight, and lengthened the time of the alarm trigger from every four hours to every 12 hours. Kathy Grady, BSN, RN, Nursing Informatics, worked with IT personnel to revise the algorithm so the alert would fire if the patient meets four of the eight criteria. (Evidence EP16-8, Sepsis Workgroup Meeting Minutes, March 30, 2016) The criteria for the sepsis alert are:

  • Male
  • Age >50 yrs.
  • Shock Index >0.7           
  • WBC risk >12 or <4       
  • SYSTOLIC SBP <90      
  • HR >95
  • MAP <60
  • Temp Risk <36 or ≥38

 

Also at the March 30, 2016 meeting, the team discussed feedback from the clinical nurses on the pilot units regarding the documentation flowsheet. Based on this feedback, the team added information into the EMR to guide the clinical nurses’ critical thinking and assessment of the patient for whom the alert was triggered. The Corporate Sepsis Workgroup responded to the clinical nurses’ request by creating a hyperlink within the alert that provides questions to consider when determining whether the patient has a new or worsening infection. (Evidence EP16-9, IT Communication Adult Sepsis Screening Alert and Hyperlink for New or Worsening Infection)

 

After all the trials and revisions were completed and approved, the alert was implemented system-wide across Beaumont Health in all appropriate inpatient nursing units in March 2017. It took a year to complete because this was a corporate initiative and many trials were performed before this final product was released. It also took time to get all parties involved in the process to sign off on the final product. 

 

The Corporate Sepsis Workgroup continues to review the data and the effectiveness of the alert. The group’s next project is to create a sepsis alert specific to the identification of a septic patient in the Emergency Center.

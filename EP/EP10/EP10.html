EP10

 

Staffing, Scheduling, and Budgeting Processes EP10

 

Nurses use trended data in the budgeting process, with clinical nurse input, to redistribute existing nursing resources or obtain additional nursing resources.

  • Provide two examples with supporting evidence from different practice settings where trended data was used during the budget process, with clinical nurse input, to assess actual-to-budget performance to redistribute existing nursing resources or to acquire additional nursing resources. Trended data must be presented.

 

 

Example One: Using Trended Data to Add a Charge Nurse (T-Code and Q-Code) to the Critical Care Unit Staffing Matrix

 

The budget process in healthcare is ongoing, with resources being distributed and redistributed to meet patient care needs. The personnel resources allocated to the Beaumont Grosse Pointe (BGP) Critical Care Unit (CCU) were evaluated and additional resources acquired based on the input of clinical nurses, the data on patient volume and Culture of Safety (COS) survey scores, and an existing staffing matrix. 

 

Practice Setting
The staffing plan for CCU included clinical nurses, clinical assistants and nursing assistants, but it did not provide for a charge nurse without a patient assignment. The clinical nurse “in charge” accepted a patient assignment along with the role of managing patient throughput for the shift and sharing miscellaneous duties such as helping clinical nurses house-wide with a variety of tasks, including challenging IV starts on midnights. The clinical nurse in charge would also respond to Rapid Response calls and code calls. A Rapid Response call is an emergency in the hospital that requires a CCU clinical nurse to be in attendance. A code is when a patient is having a cardiac emergency, and the CCU clinical nurse is part of the team that responds. CCU clinical nurses are considered a professional resource for clinical nurses throughout BGP. 

 

The CCU average daily census in 2015 and the first half of 2016 was 10 patients. The staffing plan called for 6 RNs, 1 nursing assistant and 1 clinical assistant. (Evidence EP10-1, Unit Matrix Staffing Plan, August 24, 2015) 

 

The CCU patient acuity varied and was taken into consideration when staffing decisions were made for the shift. On most shifts, however, the staffing was determined based on the staffing matrix. If the acuity of the patients was high, overtime and the Internal Resource Team would be used to supplement CCU staffing.

 

This staffing level was the center of frequent discussions between CCU clinical nurses and Kelly Adams, MSN, RN, CCU Administrative Manager. Adams frequently met with the midnight shift to discuss issues that were important to clinical nurses.  

 

Clinical Nurse Input
During one of the discussions with Shawmain Duplessis, BSN, RN and Pattie Kaminski, BSN, RN, clinical nurses on the midnight shift, they expressed concerns to Adams regarding the workload of CCU clinical nurses.

 

The issues they discussed included:

  • The impact of vacant positions
  • The impact of the Internal Resource Team nurses’ CCU experience  
  • Less experienced clinical nurses being pulled to the CCU
  • The lack of a dedicated charge nurse in the CCU

 

In preparation for the Nursing Department’s Strategic Planning Session to be held in December 2016, Anne Stewart, MBA, BSN, RN, NE-BC, Vice President and Chief Nursing Officer, sent a memo in October 2016 to solicit input from clinical nurses. In November 2016, Stewart received a reply from the CCU Unit Practice Council (UPC) regarding the need for a charge nurse on the midnight shift. This feedback validated the previous COS survey scores and comments about the need for a CCU charge nurse on the midnight shift. The UPC told Stewart:

“On our unit, we do not have a T code or a Q code (Charge RN) like the majority of the other nursing units do. This person is required to answer the designated charge phone which rings regularly, facilitate admissions, discharges, and transfers, and serve as a resource person for all other nurses, all of which is exceedingly difficult with the responsibilities of Charge RN and caring for two critically ill patients.”

Stewart shared the UPC’s memo with Adams and Reinman. (Evidence EP10-2, Memo from Unit Practice Council regarding Staffing)

 

Trended Data
Duplessis and Kaminski were aware of the turnover, especially of seasoned nurses, that was occurring in the CCU. The reasons for the turnover included resignations to move or work closer to home, transferring to other positions in anticipation of completing an advanced degree and transferring to other specialized units outside of the CCU, such as Hemodialysis or Surgical Intensive Care Unit.   

 

In addition to the staffing challenges resulting from nurse turnover, the average patient days was trending upward from fourth quarter 2015 to first quarter 2017. This data, which is the volume of patients who occupy a CCU bed at midnight averaged for the month, was consistent with the upward trend in overall volume throughout the hospital.   Although the patient days were below budget, the actual average monthly census was increasing. At BGP, the budget is used as a guideline to plan staffing, but day to day and shift to shift staffing is based on the actual census. 

 

When requesting increases in budgeted FTEs, BGP reviews actual average daily census (ADC) compared to the staffing plan. The number of nurses required for the unit is determined by the ADC and the staffing matrix, and this is compared to the budgeted FTEs. The Internal Resource Team (IRT) is used to supplement staffing for short-term census fluctuations. However, sustained increases in patient volume are addressed through adjustments to budgeted staffing numbers.

Evaluation of Data
Adams continued to monitor the census monthly and quarterly and began to see a slight increase in the number of patients. Although the census was not meeting the budget census, Adams was seeing an increase in the actual ADC. 

 

At the suggestion of Duplessis and Kaminski, Adams determined that the need for additional staffing was warranted based on the increase in the average monthly patient days. The average monthly census increased from 258 patients in fourth quarter 2015 to 282 patients in fourth quarter 2016.

 

Acquisition of Additional Nursing Resources
The budget process at Beaumont GGP is ongoing, with adjustments made throughout the year to meet units’ patient care needs. Unit staffing is based on a flex budget, which means that if the census is above budget on the unit, more staff may need to be added, and if the census is below budget, reductions in the staff may be necessary. The reduction in staffing numbers may be accomplished by not filling open positions or floating staff members to a different unit.  

 

Adams and Marie Reinman, BSN, RN, Director of Critical Care and Heart and Vascular, held multiple informal meetings to discuss clinical nurses’ feedback and the need to institute a CCU charge nurse role, which would be an additional resource for clinical nurses. The charge nurse would not normally have a patient assignment, but would serve as a support to the other CCU nurses.

 

Adams, Reinman and Stewart held a brief discussion in February 2016 that resulted in Adams obtaining approval for a charge nurse without a patient assignment on Adams’ days off.  

 

The clinical nurses gave positive feedback about having this charge nurse available on selected days, but there was still a need for the positon beyond those times. After a careful review of the staffing in CCU, and with Reinman’s guidance, it was determined that having a charge nurse in the CCU 24 hours a day, seven days a week would require 4.5 FTEs.

 

In September 2016, Adams and Reinman met with Stewart to present the request for a CCU charge nurse on day and night shifts. Along with the average patient days, COS survey scores with comments and the number of posted positions in the CCU, the clinical nurses believed their perspective was well supported.

 

After reviewing the data, Stewart approved the posting and filling of a charge nurse position on the day shift. This charge nurse position was rotated through the most seasoned clinical nurses, as identified by peers and Adams, who expressed a willingness to accept the position. In September 2016, a day shift charge nurse role was instituted in the staffing plan. (Evidence EP10-4, Email of February 16, 2016 Meeting, and Email of September 12, 2016 Meeting)

 

The day shift charge nurse position was approved in September 2016, and the charge nurse position was added to the staffing matrix for CCU. The additional staffing was submitted for the 2017 budget. Adams, believing that it was more important to the operations that the bedside clinical nurses have reasonable workloads to provide quality care to patients, decided to continue to assist with the day to day operations of the unit. 

In January 2017, a midnight shift charge nurse role was added to the staffing plan for CCU.

 

As staffing challenges were resolved, the matrix, which includes a day and midnight shift charge nurse as well as a nursing assistant and clinical assistant, has been satisfactory to the CCU clinical nurses. Once a change is made on the staffing matrix, it becomes part of the budget. The staffing matrix determines the clinical nurse FTE count for the budget. On September 7, 2016, the staffing matrix was adjusted to add a charge nurse on the day shift. On January 1, 2017, the staffing matrix was changed to add a charge nurse on the midnight shift. The current staffing matrix for the CCU has a charge nurse on both the day shift and midnight shift. 

 

The CCU’s most recent (March 2017) COS survey score for overall perception of safety was 80%, the highest score in the last five years. (Evidence EP10-5, Staffing Matrix for 2016 and 2017, FA Repot Regarding FTEs and COS Report)

 

Evaluation
The Unit Practice Council discussed the addition of charge nurses during its January 2017 meeting, where the role and responsibilities of the charge nurse were formalized.  The responsibilities of the charge nurse include:

  • Making staff assignments at the beginning and throughout the shift as needed
  • Responding to codes and RRT calls
  • Leading a safety huddle
  • Completing audits as assigned

 

Clinical nurses’ ongoing feedback was requested. CCU Clinical nurse. Necola Table, RN and Mary Kuznia, RN, CCRN provide ongoing feedback about the charge nurse role and suggestions for improving this acquired resource.

 

Example Two: Acquisition of Additional Radiology Nurse Positions

 

Practice Setting
At Beaumont Grosse Pointe (BGP), the Radiology Department employs clinical nurses in the ultrasound and CT department of General Radiology.

 

Clinical Nurse Input
On November 21, 2016, Michelle Moore, BSN, RN, clinical nurse, General Radiology, approached Heidi Kemper, MSN, RN, Administrative Manager, Interventional Heart and Vascular and Radiology Nursing, to discuss concerns regarding staffing challenges in the department. Moore was a part-time nurse in the department, and due to volumes in the department the workload was beginning to get overwhelming.

 

At the time, the department was short a 0.5 FTE, which is 20 hours per week. There was one other clinical nurse in the department, Mary Schultheis, BSN, RN, who worked a 0.5 FTE, or 20 hours per week, schedule. 

 

Moore and Schultheis worked together on some of the same days, but they most often worked opposite days, leaving only one clinical nurse in the department. In late November 2016, Schultheis gave her notice of intent to terminate her employment at BGP in early 2017. She was committed to the unit and wanted to give Kemper plenty of time to find a replacement for her. Moore expressed her concern to Kemper about this and its ramification for the clinical nurse staffing in the Radiology Department. The concerns that Moore expressed were:

  • Increased volume
  • Lack of available clinical nurses
  • Lack of flexibility of her schedule due to her commitment to school

(Evidence EP10-6, Notes of Meeting between Moore and Kemper)

 

Trended Data
Moore and Kemper had held numerous discussions over the previous six months (February-November 2016) regarding the increased volumes in the Radiology Department. Moore had kept a tally of the volumes in the department, and she presented to Kemper various trended data on its increased volume.

 

The data below outlines the trended data for the CT Scan and Ultrasound sections of the Radiology Department. These are the sections of the department that are most likely to require a clinical nurse to assist with the procedure. The actual to budgeted procedures were above budget in all 11 months of 2016.

 

The other trended data that Kemper reviewed was clinical nurse procedures, which are procedures that require a nurse to be present when the procedure is being completed.   BGP does not budget for the number of procedures that require a clinical nurse to be present, as the budget is based on the patient presenting and the procedure that is being completed. Kemper’s review of the trended data presented demonstrated that the number of procedures for which a clinical nurse is required to be present had increased in each of the 11 months of 2016 over those months in 2015.

Based on the review of the trended data from the Financial Analyst, Kemper agreed that there was a definite need for two clinical nurses in General Radiology on a daily basis. Kemper agreed that adding another FTE would help solve volume, flow and process issues in the Radiology Department.

 

Acquired Resources
The budget process at BGP is ongoing, with adjustments made throughout the year to meet the patient care needs of the areas. The area’s staffing is based on a flex budget; if the procedure count is above the budget, staffing is evaluated to determine whether an increase in FTEs is warranted. Staffing is adjusted on a day to day basis; however, budget positions are completed annually during the budget process. If there is a need to adjust the budget, compelling data, such as the data above, must be presented to the Vacancy Review Council.   

 

On November 22, 2016, Kemper met with Mark LeBlanc, RT(R) (CT) (MR), Manager, Diagnostic Imaging, and Robert Kilpatrick, BSRS, TRT, Administrative Director, to discuss the staffing challenges in Radiology and review the trended volume.

 

After a lengthy discussion, they agreed that the data and numbers provided supported the request to fill a new position. Kilpatrick requested that Kemper and LeBlanc work together to complete the paperwork for the request to fill a new position. 

 

The request to fill a new position was completed by Kemper and LeBlanc and presented to Kilpatrick for approval. The request was for an additional 0.5 FTE clinical nurse that would complement the existing 1.0 FTE clinical nurse. The staffing complement would be 1 full-time FTE clinical nurse and 1 part-time clinical nurse, to bring the total to 1.5 FTE clinical nurses.

 

The request to fill a new or vacant position is reviewed by the Vacancy Review Council to ensure the volume supports the request. Kemper and LeBlanc presented the request to the Vacancy Review Council on December 13, 2016, and it was approved by Kilpatrick and Anne Stewart, MBA, BSN, RN, NE-BC, Vice President and Chief Nursing Officer. (Evidence EP10-8, Request to Fill a New Position)

 

The job was posted on December 8, 2016. (Evidence EP10-9, Screen Shot of Clinical Nurse Job Posting)   

Kemper and Mary Smith, BSN, RN, clinical nurse, Breast Care Center, interviewed the candidates. An internal candidate was selected for the position, but she said she would only take a 0.8 FTE position rather than the 0.6 FTE position that was originally requested. Kemper spoke to Kilpatrick about this, and Kilpatrick agreed to the additional 0.2 FTE. Elbadawi, the internal candidate was offered a 0.8 FTE, and Moore was offered a 0.9 FTE. (Evidence EP10-10, Job Posting and Email Approving Additional 0.2 FTE)

 

Trended data was used, with the input of the clinical nurse, during the budget process, resulting in the Radiology Department acquiring additional nursing resources.

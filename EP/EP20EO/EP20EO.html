EP20EO

 

Culture of Safety EP20EO

 

Clinical nurses are involved in the review, action planning, and evaluation of patient safety data at the unit level.

  • Provide two examples, with supporting evidence, of an improvement in patient safety that resulted from clinical nurses' involvement in the evaluation of patient safety data at the unit level. Supporting evidence must be submitted in the form of a graph with a data table that clearly displays the data.

 

 

Example One: Decreasing the CAUTI Rate on 2 South

 

Background/Problem
Multiple recent studies have shown that the use of indwelling urinary catheters increases the risk of acquiring a catheter-associated urinary tract infection (CAUTI). Despite the sterile insertion of catheters, CAUTIs regularly occur in the acute care setting. A hospital-acquired CAUTI is financially costly to an organization due to increased length of stay with a lack of reimbursement and an increased possibility of untimely readmission.

 

A CAUTI is also costly to the patient physically, with increased morbidity and possibly mortality due to sepsis. The evidence demonstrates that the only reliable and sustainable way to reduce the incidence of CAUTIs is to reduce the use of catheters. Based on the evidence, organizations across the country have established nurse-driven protocols for the removal of indwelling urinary catheters in the absence of established criteria for their continued need. Aggressive implementation of these protocols was associated with lower catheter use and reduced infection rates.

 

CAUTIs are one of the nurse-sensitive quality measures reported to NDNQI quarterly. Kim Little, BSN, RN, Assistant Nurse Manager, and Pam Lively, BSN, RN, Assistant Nurse Manager, reviewed the NDNQI results for fourth quarter 2015 and noted that the CAUTI rate for 2 South, which had been at 0 for the first, second and third quarters, had increased to a 7.63 in fourth quarter 2015. The CAUTI rate is measured by the number of catheter-associated urinary tract infections per 1,000 catheter days

 

Little and Lively conducted extensive reviews of the cases in which a CAUTI had been identified, and they noted that in each instance the Foley had been placed prior to admission to 2 South. 

 

Little and Lively discussed this with 2 South clinical nurses Beth Thomas, RN; Emily Gross, BSN, RN; and Sharon Mysierowicz, RN and believed that the patients were frequently coming to 2 South from the Emergency Center (EC) with the indwelling urinary catheters in place. This was discussed with the EC clinical nurses, who explained that this was an efficient method for collecting urine samples upon arrival to the EC. 

 

Patients were also being transferred to 2 South from the Critical Care Stepdown Unit (CCSU) and the Critical Care Unit (CCU) with indwelling urinary catheters, as this was part of protocol for critically ill patients.

 

In both of these cases, as part of the Beaumont Grosse Pointe (BGP) nurse-driven indwelling urinary catheters protocol, the expectation is that the Foley should be discontinued before the transfer to an inpatient medical/surgical unit. Little and Lively, with the input of clinical nurses Thomas, Gross and Mysierowicz, decided that action needed to be taken at the unit level to eliminate CAUTIs on 2 South. They discussed enlisting the assistance of the Unit Practice Council to work on a process improvement plan to decrease the number of indwelling urinary catheters on the unit, thereby decreasing the number of CAUTIs.

 

The CAUTI rate on 2 South in fourth quarter 2015 was 7.63, as measured by the number of catheter-associated urinary tract infections per 1,000 catheter days.

 

Goal Statement
To decrease the CAUTI rate on 2 South.

 

Description of the Intervention/Initiative/Activities
January 2016
Little and Lively met with Gael Rodgers, BSN, RN, Clinical Infection Preventionist, and Laura Gleason, BSN, RN, Clinical Infection Preventionist. They reviewed each CAUTI case on 2 South to follow the sequence of events leading up to it. Documentation revealed that the indwelling urinary catheters had been placed prior to admission to 2 South and should have been discontinued before the patient’s arrival to the unit. Further, the staff on 2 South did not proactively discontinue the indwelling urinary catheters upon arrival or within the calendar day. Gleason and Rodgers therefore agreed that, per policy, the CAUTIs would be attributed to 2 South even though the unit was not initially responsible for the deviation from protocol. Little and Lively discussed the probability that staff members were not aware of the critical nature of timely intervention.  

 

The information was shared and evaluated with the 2 South Unit Practice Council (UPC) at its next scheduled meeting. The council members, clinical nurses Thomas; Annette Gipperich, RN; Timothy Pierce, BSN, RN; and Michael Hoekstra, BSN, RN, discussed the findings and determined that something must be done to prevent further CAUTIs on 2 South. As the UPC members were not aware of the importance of removing the Foley in a timely manner, they decided that education should be provided to the clinical nurses.

 

The UPC members decided that to decrease the number of indwelling urinary catheters on the unit, they had to stop them before these patients were admitted to the unit. They decided that they would review the criteria for an indwelling urinary catheter when the clinical nurses from EC, CCSU and CCU call to transfer the patient.

 

The clinical nurses were encouraged, as they accepted a transfer from the EC, CCSU or CCU, to question and research the need for the indwelling urinary catheter if the patient had one in place. They were instructed to ask the transferring nurse to remove the indwelling urinary catheter if not clinically indicated. The only clinical indications for the indwelling urinary catheter to remain in place were:

  • Bladder dysfunction and urinary retention
  • Accurate fluid intake and output for CCU
  • Short-term use for surgery
  • Management of incontinence of patients with sacral wounds
  • Bladder irrigation
  • Epidural catheter in place for pain control
  • Relieve urinary tract obstruction
  • Fracture of spine, pelvis or lower extremity

 

They were also encouraged to review the EC timeline prior to the patient’s admission to the unit to determine whether the order and the criteria were accurate.

 

January 2016
Little and Lively began sharing their findings one-on-one with the clinical nurses each day. The information was shared electronically, included in the UPC minutes and discussed at staff meetings.

 

February 2016
Little and Lively discussed the importance of timely adherence to the nurse-driven protocol with each of the off shift charge nurses (clinical nurses Judy Thueme, RN;  Sandra Kanas, RN; Susan Zabel, BSN, RN; MaryEllen Riker, RN; Tricia Giles, RN; and Rebecca Ryan, RN). The charge nurses were asked to monitor for indwelling urinary catheters every day at midnight.

 

The charge nurses were expected to question the clinical nurses caring for the patient to determine whether criteria for the continuation of the indwelling urinary catheter were met. If the criteria were not met, the nurses were to discontinue the catheter. Little and Lively discussed indwelling urinary catheters placement with each nurse during daily Care Management Rounds.

 

March 2016
Throughout March, the UPC champions, charge nurses and Assistant Nurse Managers made concerted and continued efforts to engage each clinical nurse in 2 South’s work to eliminate CAUTIs. By the end of March, the staff had begun to incorporate the adherence to the protocol into their daily plans of care, and they began to feel comfortable discussing indwelling urinary catheters placement with all nurses at BGP.

Example Two: Decreasing Falls on the Critical Care Stepdown Unit by Implementing Hourly Rounding

 

Background/Problem
Keeping patients safe is a cornerstone of nurses’ practice. Despite best practices, and policies and procedures designed to prevent patient falls, these events still occur.

 

In late December 2016, Sue Wolka, BSN, RN, NE-BC, Administrative Manager, Critical Care Stepdown Unit (CCSU), was reviewing data on the number of falls that had occurred on the unit. Wolka tracked the data weekly and noted an increase in the number of falls. The CCSU fall rate for December 2016 was 2.99, measured by the number of falls per 1,000 patient days. Wolka decided to involve the CCSU Unit Practice Council in developing a plan to address the problem.

 

Wolka met with Melissa Haezebrouck, BSN, RN, clinical nurse and chairperson of the CCSU Unit Practice Council (UPC), at the end of December 2016 to discuss the issue and ask that the UPC develop a program to decrease the number of falls. 

 

The CCSU nurses recognized the need to decrease the number of patient falls. They were also aware that patients’ perception of nurses’ responsiveness to their needs was falling short of what was expected.

Recognizing an opportunity to improve patients’ perception of prompt attention to their needs while decreasing the number of patient falls, the clinical nurses were empowered to develop a plan to achieve these goals.

 

The CCSU fall rate for December 2016 was 2.99, measured by the number of falls per 1,000 patient days.  

 

Goal Statement
To decrease the fall rate on the Critical Care Stepdown Unit.

 

Description of the Intervention/Initiative/Activities
All interventions were implemented in January and February 2017.

 

January 2017
During the January UPC meeting, the nurses discussed the data on the number of falls on CCSU for the previous six months:

  • July 2016 – 3 falls
  • August 2016 – 2 falls
  • September 2016 – 2 falls
  • October 2016 – 3 falls
  • November 2016 – 2 falls
  • December 2016 – 3 falls

 

The data demonstrated that the CCSU nurses needed to take action to reduce patient falls in the CCSU. The UPC discussed this and decided to investigate the issue and possible solutions and develop a plan for decreasing falls at the February meeting. 

 

February 8, 2017
The UPC members identified the opportunity to reduce patient falls on the unit and explored ways of putting a fall reduction program into place. Allyce Grover, RN, clinical nurse, CCSU, had previously worked at Beaumont Grosse Point’s (BGP) sister hospital, Beaumont Troy. Grover noted that her previous unit had successfully used hourly rounding on all patients to decrease the number of patient falls. Grover said all nursing staff members were expected to engage in hourly rounding or have a substitute rounder if they were unable to round at their assigned time.

 

Using Grover’s account of hourly rounding as an example, the UPC members developed a plan for CCSU:

  • The unit would be divided into two sections: Rooms 230-239 would be considered the front of the unit and rooms 240-250 would be considered the back.
  • One caregiver would be assigned to round on the front half of the unit and a second caregiver would be assigned to round on the back half.
  • Hourly rounding would consist of going to each patient’s bedside and asking them about the fours “P’s”: Pain, Positioning, Potty, Possessions. The nurses would ask the patients:
  • Do you need anything for pain?
  • Are you comfortable in the your bed or do you need to be repositioned?
  • Do you have to go the bathroom?
  • Do you need anything moved closer to your bedside?

 

At the UPC meeting, the nurses created a rounding sheet including a script to help staff members ensure all patient needs were met.

 

Grover suggested adding the question, “Is there anything else I can do for you before I leave?” to give patients another opportunity to voice any other questions or concerns outside of the four “P’s”. Both day and night shift nurses would be expected to take part in the hourly rounding program.

 

At the end of the meeting, the UPC members met with Wolka, who supported this plan.

 

February 10, 2017
Haezebrouck sent a unit-wide email explaining to all nursing staff members the concept of hourly rounding, staff responsibilities and expectations, and guidelines. The email included two evidence-based practice articles supplied by Grover detailing the benefits of hourly rounding and clinical nurses’ role in reducing patient falls.

 

February 13, 2017
A CCSU staff meeting was held to further explain roles and responsibilities and the hourly rounding program, and for staff members to express any questions or concerns. It was decided that the hourly rounding program would begin on February 20, 2017.

 

February 13-14, 2017
To gather additional data on the impact of the hourly rounding program, the nurses had a report created to capture the number of call lights that were put on during the 24 hours prior to the implementation of the rounding program; this was 190. They plan to monitor this over time to see whether there is a decrease in call lights after the implementation of hourly rounding.

 

February 20-28, 2017
The implementation of all interventions was complete and the hourly rounding program on CCSU began on March 1, 2017.

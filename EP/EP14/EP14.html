EP14

 

Accountability, Competence, and Autonomy EP14

 

Resources, such as professional literature, are readily available to support decision-making in autonomous nursing practice.

  • Provide two examples, with supporting evidence, of how resources are used to support evidence-based clinical decision-making in autonomous nursing practice.

 

 

Example One: Evidence-based Resources to Improve Stroke Care at Beaumont Grosse Pointe

 

Internationally recognized research has determined that early recognition and treatment of stroke gives patients the best possible outcomes. Quality and timely care for patients presenting to the Beaumont Grosse Pointe (BGP) Emergency Center (EC) with stroke symptoms relies on several factors.

 

Evidence-based Research
Staff members quickly performing a clinical assessment to determine that a patient is possibly suffering a stroke and then providing high-priority care is the key to early diagnosis, treatment and differentiation between ischemic and hemorrhagic stroke.

 

According to the article, “The Golden Hour; Performing an Acute Ischemic Stroke Workup” by J. Anderson in the journal The Nurse Practitioner, appropriate evaluation and treatment with IV fibrinolysis therapy can make a critical difference between independence and disability. (Evidence EP14-1, Article The Golden Hour, Performing an Acute Ischemic Stroke Workup)

 

Resources Used
To improve the overall response time to the care and treatment of possible stoke patients, clinical nurses in the EC at BPG worked closely with Cathleen Solecki, MSN, RN-BC, CCRN, CNRN, Stroke Coordinator, and the Stroke Team made up of clinical nurses from throughout BGP, to develop processes for improvement. EC clinical nurses used the Beaumont Clinical Practice Guidelines as a resource to establish processes and protocols to ensure rapid triage with a fast assessment of clinical symptoms.

 

BGP developed an Emergency Center and In-House Acute Stroke Algorithm that outlines the protocol to follow when working with an acute possible stroke patient. The algorithm outlines the care that should be completed when the patient is first identified as a stroke patient. The algorithm outlines the following steps:

  • Within 5 minutes make initial assessment based on last know well
  • Within 10 minutes physician must see patient
  • Within 15 minutes code stroke is called
  • Within 25 minutes patient is in radiology for CT scan
  • Within 45 minutes results of CT scan are available
  • Within 60 minutes tPA is given if indicated

 

Neurological signs and symptoms are quickly identified, and last known well (LKW) is documented. The LKW is the last time the caregiver can definitely say that the patient was functioning normally. Once the LKW is verified, the Emergency Severity Index (ESI) tool is used to appropriately identify a patient as high priority. The ESI is a five-level EC  triage algorithm that provides clinically relevant stratification of patients into one of five groups, from 1 (most urgent) to 5 (least urgent), on the basis of acuity and resource needs.

 

The Clinical Practice Guidelines are based on research provided by the American Heart Association and American Stroke Association. Three practice changes were made as a result of the collaboration between EC clinical nurses, Solecki and the multidisciplinary Stroke Team:

  • A Code Stroke may be initiated by the clinical nurse.
  • All possible stroke patients with an LKW of 12 hours or less would be given an ESI score of 1 and thereby treated as a high priority patient.
  • Treatment for patients presenting to the EC with stroke-like symptoms was adjusted to ensure faster recognition of ischemic stroke.
    (Evidence EP14-2, Emergency Center and In-House Acute Stroke Algorithm)

 

Change in Practice
After significant collaboration with the Stroke Team, the EC nursing leadership team looked further into nurse-driven steps that could be incorporated into the EC clinical nurse’ practice. A resource that was available but rarely used by the EC RNs was the stroke protocol Advanced Treatment Guideline (ATG) - Stroke Adult.

 

The ATG includes signs and symptoms of a possible stroke, parameters for ESI scoring based on LKW, and diagnostic and treatment modalities including the order in which they should be done. Based on the triage assessment, the EC clinical nurse would do the following:

  • Assess for signs and symptoms of stroke
  • Establish last know well
  • Perform blood glucose
  • Order head CT scan
  • Obtain EKG, place on pulse oximetry and place patient on monitor
  • Secure IV access
  • Obtain other diagnostic testing

 

During its meetings, the Stroke Team discussed ways in which BGP would change its practice to better serve patients. For patients presenting with neurological symptoms and a LKW of 12 hours or less, the nurse would autonomously initiate the Code Stroke before seeing the physician, and facilitate moving the patient to CT before establishing an IV or doing an EKG. (Evidence EP14-3, Policy 1010 Stroke- Adult; September 1, 2016 Meeting Minutes of Stroke Team)

 

Using the Beaumont Health Advanced Treatment Guidelines, in conjunction with EC and Neurology physicians, the EC clinical nurses are empowered to use the resources available to them to make the autonomous decision to initiate a Code Stroke to improve outcomes for patients presenting to the BGP Emergency Center.  

 

Autonomous Decision-making in Clinical Practice
On May 21, 2017, patient M.L. arrived at the BGP Emergency Center at 10:35 a.m. with symptoms of a stroke. The EC clinical nurse called a Code Stroke based on the information the nurse had received from Emergency Medical Service (EMS) from the field; the LWK time was 10:10 a.m. The following is stroke care provided, in chronological order, by the clinical nurses, physicians and ancillary care providers at BGP.

 

Prior to the patient arriving to the Emergency Center, the clinical nurses autonomously called the Code Stroke, which summoned the Stroke Team to the EC. Based on the protocol, the clinical nurse can independently call a Code Stroke.

 

Once the patient arrived, Ashely Brooke Meier, BSN, RN, clinical nurse, Emergency Center, determined that a blood sugar and vital signs were not required at the time since they had just been completed by EMS. 

 

Between 10:36 and 10:40 a.m., Meier and the physician completed their assessment.  Based on Meier’s assessment, the patient showed the following symptoms:

  • Mumbled noise when patient tried to talk
  • Confusion
  • Right sided weakness
  • Right sided facial droop

 

Meier autonomously determined that the patient was showing signs and symptoms of a stroke, so she sent MD to the Radiology Department at 10:42 a.m. for a head CT scan without contrast.

 

At 11:00 a.m., Meier completed the nursing swallow screen per protocol. The swallow screen includes the following assessment points:

  • Are there past or present signs or symptoms of a stroke, TIA, or neurological impairment?
  • Is the patient alert, cooperative and able to follow simple commands?
  • Upon completion of the adult nursing swallow screen for the administration of oral medications, M.L. failed the swallow screen so Meier made M.L. nothing by mouth (NPO).

 

Based on the assessments of Meier, the CT tech and physician, tPA was given to M.L. 

EC clinical nurse Meier followed the stroke care protocol ,and by 11:57 a.m. the patient reported an improvement in their speech and strength. (Evidence EP14-4, EC Timeline of Care and Screen Shots from Electronic Medical Record)

 

Nurses in the Emergency Center use the evidence-based stroke protocol to support autonomous nursing care decisions for patients with acute stroke symptoms.

 

Example Two: Using the Transcutaneous Bilimeter to Promote Autonomous Practice in the Family Birth Center

 

Evidence-based Resources
The American Academy of Pediatrics (AAP) clinical practice guideline on the management of hyperbilirubinemia in the newborn  recommends, that prior to discharge, all infants be assessed for the risk of subsequent severe hyperbilirubinemia by measuring the total serum bilirubin (TsB) or trancutaneous bilirubin (TcB) and plotting the value on a nomogram. This information is valuable because it gives the clinical nurse caring for a newborn in the hospital setting a simple and relevant measure of the risk of subsequent development of hyperbilirubinemia.

 

In August 2013, as part of the effort to allow the clinical nurses in the Family Birth Center (FBC) at Beaumont Grosse Pointe (BGP) to care for newborns autonomously, the Transcutaneous Bilirubin/Jaundice Meter policy was updated to reflect the most recent research from Jeffery Maisels, MD, Chief of Pediatrics, Beaumont Health, related to the monitoring of bilirubin levels in the newborn and the appropriate follow-up. (Evidence EP14-5, Policy 6346 Transcutaneous Bilirubin/Jaundice Meter) 

 

The guidelines n this policy were developed following the realization that institutions were struggling to follow the AAP recommendations. The AAP recommendations are to:

  • Screen all newborns prior to discharge for elevated bilirubin levels
  • Follow up for all newborn based TcB levels, gestational age and other risk factors

 

The purpose of the guidelines and updated policy was to reduce the incidence of severe hyperbilirubinemia and resulting bilirubin encephalopathy. Maisels’ research and recommendations included performing universal predischarge bilirubin screening using TsB or TcB measurements to assess the risk of subsequent severe hyperbilirubinemia. (Evidence EP14-6, Article Hyperbilirubinemia in the Newborn Infant ) 35 Weeks Gestation: An Update with Clarifications and –Routine Transcutaneous Bilirubin Measurement Combined with Clinical Risk Factors Improve the Prediction of Subsequent Hyperbilirubinemia)

 

The recommendations that were built into the policy included a more structured approach to the management and follow-up based on the pre-discharge TsB/TcB, gestational age and other hyperbilirubinemia risk factors. 

 

Resource
Policy 6346 Transcutaneous Bilirubin/Jaundice Meter was updated by Audrey Kalask, MSN, RN, PNP-BC, Clinical Nurse Specialist, Beaumont Royal Oak NICU, and Brian Berman, MD, Health System Chair, Pediatrics. It was then circulated and appproved by the Administrative Manager, Clinical Nurse Specialist and Pediatric Chiefs of Beaumont Grosse Pointe, Beaumont-Royal Oak and Beaumont Troy.

 

Once the policy was approved, all clinical nurses caring for newborns on the BGP Family Birth Center were educated on the use of the Transcutaneous Bilimeter JM-103. 

 

Change in Clinical Practice
In September 2013, based on Maisels’ work on hyperbilirubinemia, bilirubin plotting on a nomogram and the corresponding follow-up guidelines were automated in oneChart.

 

In addition to assisting the clinical nurse to assess the infant’s risk of developing subsequent hyperbilirubinemia, this feature includes management and follow-up guidelines, phototherapy guidelines, exchange transfusion guidelines, and training and references. (Evidence EP14-7, IT Communication Bilirubin Nomogram)

 

This gives the clinical nurse immediate access to relevant clinical guidelines. The bilirubin nomogram is accessible from the rounding, admission, shift and discharge navigators of Epic (BGP’s electronic medical record), and is visible for providers in the office visit navigator and telephone encounter navigator.

 

The clinical nurse caring for the newborn on the BGP Family Birth Center follows the physician’s order within the IP Newborn Admission Protocol-FBC/LDRP order set and the Transcutaneous Bilirubin/Jaundice Meter Policy.

 

The clinical nurse is autonomous in caring for the infant. She or he uses this resource and the data from the TcB reading to care for the infant using evidence-based practice. Once the TcB reading is entered into Epic, it is automatically plotted on the Bilirubin Nomogram graph, which takes into account the Hyperbilirubin Risk and Neurotoxicity Risk factors.

 

The TcB policy and Newborn Admission Protocol order set were instituted for all clinical nurses to autonomously care for newborns. According to the physician-to-nurse orders within the IP Newborn Admission Protocol-FBC/LDRP and the policy, the nurse orders and obtains a serum neonatal bilirubin level (TsB) if the infant is jaundiced at < 24 hours of age or if the TcB reading is >95% at any age according to the Bilirubin Nomogram. (Evidence EP14-8, Order Set-IP Newborn Admission Protocol-FBC/LDRPand TcB flowsheet and nomogram in EPIC)

 

Autonomous Decision-making In Clinical Practice
On June 13, 2017 at 0300, Christina Richard, BSN, RN, clinical nurse, Family Birth Center, as part of the routine newborn nursing care, obtained an initial TCB reading on the infant on the first day of life, at 17 hours of age. The Tcb was 10.9 and was entered in the EMR on the flowsheet.

 

The results auto-populated on the Bilirubin Nomogram graph ,and Richard noted that the result was above the 95th percentile. Richard followed the Transcutaneous Bilirubin policy and IP Newborn Admission Protocol, which allowed her to obtain and send a serum bilirubin sample. The results of the serum bilirubin were 7.3 mg/dl, which was still above the 95th percentile on the Bilirubin Nomogram. Richard reported the results to the attending pediatrician.

 

At 0900, another serum bilirubin was obtained by Judith Constant, BSN, RN, C-EFM, clinical nurse, Family Birth Center, per protocol, and the results were 9.5 at 25 hours of age, which was again above the 95th percentile. Constant notified the physican. Orders were obtained for triple phototherapy for the baby and a repeat serum bilirubin at 1800. 
(Evidence EP14-9, Screen Shot of Electronic Medical Record)

 

Nurses at BGP use evidence-based resources such as the Transcutaneous Bilirubin/Jaundice Policy to support autonomous decision-making in the care of patients.

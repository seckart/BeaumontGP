EP9

 

Staffing, Scheduling, and Budgeting Processes EP9

 

Nurses are involved in staffing and scheduling based on established guidelines, such as ANA’s Principles for Nurse Staffing, to ensure that RN assignments meet the needs of the patient population.

  • Provide two examples, with supporting evidence, from different practice settings when input from clinical nurses was used to modify RN staffing assignments and/or adjust the schedule to compensate for a change in patient acuity, patient population, resources, or redesign of care.

 

 

Example One: Modifying RN Staffing in the Critical Care Stepdown Unit Based on Patient Acuity

 

Nurses are involved in staffing and scheduling based on established guidelines, such as the American Nurses Association (ANA) Principles for Nurse Staffing, to ensure that RN assignments meet the needs of the patient populations. Proper clinical nurse staffing assists in optimizing patient outcomes.

 

Patient Population and Acuity
The Critical Care Stepdown Unit (CCSU) at Beaumont Grosse Pointe (BGP) is a 39-bed cardiac telemetry unit with the capability of admitting up to six ventilator-dependent patients. The unit can admit three ventilator patients without blocking additional beds.  If additional ventilator beds beyond the three are necessary, a bed in a semi-private room would have to be blocked to accommodate the ventilator patient. 

 

In addition to Intensive Care Unit (ICU) patients transferring to CCSU, the CCSU has a patient population with complex, multi-faceted health issues including patients with cardiac issues, medical patients with many comorbidities, stable patients with tracheostomies and ventilated patients. Common diagnoses that are admitted to CCSU include:

  • Post-cardiac arrest
  • Paroxysmal atrial fibrillation
  • Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease with acute exacerbation

 

Clinical nurses caring for this patient population must be alert to changes in patient condition and remain diligent to properly manage the increasing acuity and complexity of care that is required.

 

The CCSU Unit Practice Council discussed staffing in March 2013 in response to increased census, including multiple ventilated patients. Before staffing could be addressed, however, the census decreased and the problem resolved itself.

 

Staffing to deliver safe, quality care to complex ventilated patients and other critical stepdown patients became a concern again in late 2013. During this time of extremely high census, an increased number of ventilated patients presented in the BGP Emergency Center. There was also an increasing number of Critical Care Unit (CCU) overflow patients present on the CCSU.

 

The CCSU staffing was typically one clinical nurse for every four to five patients. On the day shift, clinical nurses were assigned four patients, and on the night shift up to five patients. One of the patients in the clinical nurses’ assignment would frequently be either a ventilated patient or a CCU overflow patient. Because of the complexity of these patients, clinical nurses became overwhelmed with the patient care assignment.   

 

Clinical Nurse Input into Staffing
In January 2014, CCSU clinical nurses Lauri Rowland, RN; Michelle Urben, RN; and Shelia Thacker, BSN, RN described to CCSU Assistant Nurse Managers Sue Wolka, BSN, RN and Eric Quenneville, BSN, RN the need for an increase in clinical nurse staffing to provide quality care to ventilated patients and CCU overflow patients. (Evidence EP9-1, Notes from January 6, 2014 Conversation)

 

Urben described the typical care of a ventilated patient, which included:

  • Increased airway management
  • Increased turning with attention to skin integrity due to decreased mobility
  • Altered ability to effectively communicate, requiring increased time at the bedside.

 

Rowland, Urben and Thacker agreed that the current staffing plan was not adequate to take care of the stepdown patients when one was a ventilated or a CCU overflow patient. The matrix dated 10/31/2012 to 5/1/14 did not address additional staff for patients who are admitted with a ventilator. It was a straight ratio matrix. (Evidence EP9-2, 2014 Current Unit Matrix for CCSU dated 10/13/12 through 5/1/2014)

 

Urben met with a colleague from the Critical Care Unit to obtain information on the staffing patterns for critical care patients and ventilated patients in the CCU. The established patient-to-nurse ratio in CCU was two patients (maximum, ventilated patients) for one nurse (2:1 ratio). The CCU matrix was designed with the assumption that most CCU patients are on a ventilator, requiring a 2:1 patient-to-nurse ratio. The CCSU clinical nurses wanted a similar staffing matrix for ventilated patients on the CCSU. It was determined that it would be in the best interest of safe, effective care for complex acuity ventilator patients to establish more appropriate patient-to-nurse ratios in CCSU for ventilated patients. (Evidence EP9-3, CCU Staffing Matrix dated 8/15/2013)

 

Input into Resources Required
Rowland, Urben and Thacker proposed to Wolka and Quenneville that staffing should be increased based on the number of patients who require a ventilator. Clinical nurses suggested that the CCSU should have one additional clinical nurse if the unit has one to two ventilated patients, two additional clinical nurses if the unit has three to four ventilated patients and three additional clinical nurses if the unit has five to six ventilated patients. They also said the unit should have a permanent charge nurse without a patient assignment since the Assistant Nurse Managers are frequently unavailable for staffing because of their administrative responsibilities.  

 

Wolka and Quenneville agreed with Rowland, Urben and Thacker’s assessment, and they developed a plan to present the information obtained from the clinical nurses to Marie Reinman, RN, Director Critical Care and Heart and Vascular. Wolka discussed with Reinman the following points:

  • The increased number of ventilated patients
  • The increased number of CCU overflow patients
  • The increased acuity of other CCSU patients
  • Benchmarking data obtained from clinical nurses
  • What they would like to accomplish

 

In early February 2014, Wolka and Quenneville met with Reinman to discuss the clinical nurses’ concerns and to solicit Reinman’s input into how to operationalize this request.  They discussed how this was an un-budgeted staffing request that would have to be reviewed and approved by Anne Stewart, MBA, BSN, RN, NE-BC, Vice President and Chief Nursing Officer.

 

Reinman offered the following concerns regarding the proposal:

  • How would CCSU staff this position?
  • How frequently would additional staff be required?
  • What would happen if the staff is hired and the volume decreases?

 

Reinman suggested that Wolka take these questions back to clinical nurses and develop talking points for when they met with Stewart.

 

Staffing Modification for Higher Acuity
In late February 2014, Wolka and Reinman met with Stewart to review the proposed changes to the CCSU staffing matrix, including how CCSU was going to staff the additional nurses and what would happen if the volume decreased. They determined that the unit would staff for at least one ventilator patient per day. Stewart approved the proposed change that had been developed with clinical nurses’ input. 

 

They also discussed the clinical nurses’ proposal to have a charge nurse without a patient assignment, which was not approved at the time.

 

On March 25, 2014, the CCSU staffing matrix was modified with the addendum about the increased staffing when ventilated patients are on the unit. (Evidence EP9-4, Staffing Matrix dated 3/24/14)

 

The enhanced staffing matrix stayed in place in CCSU through 2016, and further modifications were made based on continued clinical nurse input. During the 2015-2016 budget season for fiscal year 2016, a charge nurse was added to the staffing matrix on the day shift without a patient assignment. This request of the clinical nurses from 2014 was now approved in 2016.  

 

One example of the use of the CCSU staffing matrix comes from October 26, 2016. CCSU had 33 patients, with one ventilated patient on the unit in room 248. The following provision of the unit matrix was used to determine staffing:

“When staffing up for vent volume, be sure to adjust total matrix. For example, if total census is 28, but there are 5 vents, the 23 non-vented patients would require 6 clinical nurses with an additional 3 for the vented patients – not 3 additional to the matrix of 7 for 28 patients.”

On October 26, 2016, the unit had 27 non-vented patients and one vented patient. Based on the unit matrix, the unit should have eight clinical nurses plus one additional nurse for the vented patient. There was also a charge nurse without a patient assignment. (Evidence EP9-5, Assignment Sheet and Staffing Matrix for October 26, 2016)

 

Ventilated patients continue to be admitted to the CCSU when the Critical Care Unit is full or the patient does not meet critical care criteria. CCSU is the only other unit at BGP that is able to accept ventilated patients. However, clinical nurses are provided additional resources when the acuity on the unit increases due to ventilated patients.

 

Based on clinical nurses’ input into staffing adjustments in 2014, the unit acquired additional clinical nurse resources and an unassigned charge nurse during the day shift based on the acuity of the patients.

 

 

Example Two: Adjusting Scheduling for Emergency Center Clinical Nurses in Response to Increases in Patient Population Volume  

 

Beaumont Health’s mission is to provide Compassionate, Extraordinary Care Every Day. The focus is on the patients and families cared for. Beaumont Health also places great importance on caring for and supporting clinical nurses to meet this mission. Beaumont Health has an open-door leadership policy, which enables clinical nurses to provide input into safety, staffing and leadership.   

 

Clinical Nurse Input
Staff Meetings
In August 2014, Emergency Center (EC) clinical nurses discussed staffing problems with EC nurse leaders. With the volume increase, clinical nurses were informing nurse leaders that additional RN staffing was needed and a change in scheduling of RN staff should be considered.

 

Based on the concerns that the clinical nurses were expressing regarding the staffing challenges due to increased volume, the nurse leaders agreed to give a presentation during the August staff meeting on the status of the staffing in the EC. (Evidence EP9-6, Staff Meeting Minutes, August 26 and 27, 2014 and Attendance Roster)

 

In 2014, the average daily census (ADC) was increasing. The ADC in the Emergency Center is defined as the average number of patients seen in a calendar day. The ADC was 95.3 patients in January 2014 and increased to 111.6 in July 2014. This was a 17.1% increase in the number of patients seen in the EC daily.  

 

EC clinical nurses also believed that adjusting shift times would assist with the increased patient flow. They agreed that the EC should have three additional shifts to help cover the peak demand in the census. The shifts they decided upon were 9 a.m.- 9 p.m., 1 p.m.-1 a.m. and 3 p.m. to 3 a.m. 

 

Satisfaction Surveys – Culture of Safety and NDNQI Nurse Satisfaction
One way in which BGP creates a free environment in which to discuss staffing-related issues is through the Agency for Healthcare Research and Quality (AHRQ) Culture of Safety (COS) Survey. This survey, conducted annually, gives clinical nurses an opportunity to provide input into staffing. The survey measures the clinical staff’s perception of various topics to gauge how they feel about the safety of the unit. One of the items on the COS survey completed in December 2014 was, “We have enough staff to handle the workload.” In December 2014, 31% of EC staff members indicated that the EC had enough staff to handle the workload, a decrease from the December 2013 score of 46%. The 2014 score was below the AHRQ average of 44%.

 

EC clinical nurses also completed the NDNQI RN Satisfaction Survey in 2014. One of the sections in the survey was Staffing and Resource Adequacy. BGP uses the following benchmarks when reviewing the results:

  • Compared by: Teaching Status
  • Peer Group: Teaching Facilities
  • Unit Type: Emergency Centers 30,000-49,999 Annual Visits
  • Number of Units: 24

 

The EC’s score for Staffing and Resource Adequacy was 2.08, compared to the mean score of the benchmarking group of 2.32 and the median of 2.21.

 

Based on the COS and NDNQI scores, it was evident that something needed to be done to address the staffing concerns of EC clinical nurses. (Evidence EP9-7, Emergency Center COS Survey Results, page 15, and 2014 NDNQI RN Survey Staffing and Resource Adequacy)

 

The clinical nurses’ input was reviewed by the Emergency Center leadership team, which included Wendy Jolley, BSN, RN, Director, and Charlotta Marion, RN, Assistant Nurse Manager. Jolley decided that something had to be done in response to the concerns about staffing that EC leadership was hearing and how unsafe nurses felt with the nurse-to-patient ratio. Based on Jolley’s review of the COS and NDNQI data and the conversations she was having with clinical nurses during her rounding, she realized the issues needed to be addressed quickly. The nurse leaders discussed various ideas and process improvements, and they were aware that staff engagement and participation would be necessary for a successful outcome.

 

Marion identified trends in the comments about RN staffing, including more clinical nurses being needed to safely care for patients. Marion spoke with clinical nurses Beverly Gandolfi, RN; Celeste Decker, BSN, RN; and Brendon Franklin, RN about the staffing issues in the EC. These clinical nurses said that with the increased volume of patients entering the EC, they believed there was a need to increase the number of clinical nurses to ensure they could continue to follow the mission and standards of Beaumont Health.

 

Gandolfi was a senior clinical nurse and had experienced many staffing changes in the department. She believed that even with the positive changes over the years, the increase in census and the perceived increase in the acuity of patients meant that additional staffing was needed to provide the high-quality healthcare that Beaumont prides itself on. Based on her experience, Gandolfi knew that patients were presenting in the EC with a higher acuity, but this could not be confirmed since BGP’s EC does not have an acuity system. In addition, the volume in the EC has steadily increased over the years:

  • 2013: 28,517 EC Visits
  • 2014: 30,729 EC Visits
  • 2015: 30,813 EC Visits
  • 2016: 42,442 EC Visits
    (Evidence EP9-8, Volumes Growth 2010 to 2016 Budgeted)

 

Scheduling Adjustment
In November 2014,after speaking with Gandolfi, Decker and Franklin, Marion scheduled an appointment with Christine Stesney-Ridenour, MBA, Vice President and Chief Operating Officer, regarding clinical nurse staffing in the Emergency Center. Marion explained to Stesney-Ridenour the barriers to hiring clinical nurses for the EC. Stesney-Ridenour told Marion to do what she needed to hire additional ED nurses and that she would speak with Human Resources to provide the support needed to do so.  Based on the volumes in the EC, paid FTEs increased from 33.37 FTEs in 2013 to 42.45 FTEs in 2016. (Evidence EP9-9, Clinical Nurse FTEs Over Time)

 

The EC nursing leadership team continued to see an increase in the ADC in 2014 and 2015. In December 2014,based on previous discussion with clinical nurses, Jolley held additional informal discussions with clinical nurses about the alternative schedule, and most agreed that it would be something they could try. The goal was to match the staffing with the census. Jolley spoke with Gandolfi since she was one of the senior clinical nurses and had provided information previously, and Gandolfi thought it was a great idea.

 

The leadership team, with input from clinical nurses, decided that the EC would adjust scheduling by implementing different shifts, allowing for more nursing staff during the typically busiest hours of the day. The new schedules began in 2015.The new shifts were:

  • 0900-1900
  • 1300-0100
  • 1500-0300

 

The staff matrix outlines the number of nurses that are needed each day of the week at the various times of the day. The matrix also contains three sections, A, B and C, to ensure that there are adequate nurses available to cover the shifts seven days a week. The position control sheet identifies the clinical nurses who are in the various shifts to cover the staffing requirements for the EC.  The position control sheet identifies the new shifts that were implemented based on the input from the clinical nurses. (Evidence EP9-10, Position Control)   

 

The staffing by hour in the Emergency Center is outlined below:

Clinical nurses and the leadership team noted that, with these changes and with nurses hired into these shifts, more nurses were available to care for patients during peak volume times. Midnight nurses reported more coverage to assist during the busy times of the night, and decreasing staff at 3 a.m. was in line with the time of the morning that census began to decrease.

 

Clinical nurses began to accept the new shifts and appreciate the fact that leadership was responding to their input. This was reflected by an increase in the Culture of Safety score related to staffing. The COS score for the item, “We have enough staff to handle the workload” increased from 31% in December 2014 to 38% in December 2015 and an all-time high of 60% in December 2016. The NDNQI RN Satisfaction Survey score for Staffing and Resource Adequacy also increased, from 2.08 in 2014 to 2.47 in 2015.

Beaumont’s mission statement is Compassionate, Extraordinary Care Every Day. This is a mission the EC strives to uphold every day, every shift and by every team member. EC leadership values clinical nurses’ input to uphold this mission.
